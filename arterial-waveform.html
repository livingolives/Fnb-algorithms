Arterial Waveform Physiology

Transducer Setup · Waveform Components · Shock-Specific Changes

📈 Normal Waveform Anatomy

Normal Arterial Pressure Waveform
Upstroke slope = contractility (dP/dt). Dicrotic notch position/height = vascular tone (high/close to peak = vasoconstricted; low/absent = vasodilated). Area under curve ≈ stroke volume (basis of pulse contour CO monitoring). Diastolic decay rate reflects SVR.

🔧 Transducer Setup — Physics

Zeroing
Transducer opened to air, zeroed to atmospheric pressure — removes atmospheric pressure from the measurement, so displayed value is pressure above atmospheric.
Levelling — Phlebostatic Axis
Transducer positioned at the level of the right atrium (4th ICS, mid-axillary line). Too high → falsely low reading. Too low → falsely high reading — each cm of height error ≈ 0.75 mmHg.
Square Wave (Flush) Test
Fast-flush the line, observe the return to baseline. Optimally damped = 1-2 oscillations before settling. Underdamped = many oscillations, overshoots (bubbles, stiff long tubing) → falsely high systolic. Overdamped = sluggish, no oscillation, rounded waveform (clot, kink, air) → falsely low systolic, narrowed pulse pressure.
Natural Frequency & Damping Coefficient
System natural frequency should be far above the frequency content of the arterial pulse to avoid resonance artifact. Short, stiff, bubble-free tubing minimises resonance and maximises fidelity.

🩸 Waveform Changes — Haemorrhagic vs Septic Shock

Haemorrhagic Shock
Narrow pulse pressure — SBP falls, DBP relatively preserved/rises (compensatory vasoconstriction) → pulse pressure narrows.
Exaggerated respiratory swing — large PPV on the arterial trace with ventilation, reflecting preload dependence (fluid responsive).
Low dicrotic notch height, close to peak — high SVR from compensatory vasoconstriction.
Steep diastolic runoff initially masked by vasoconstriction, becomes rapid as compensation fails.
Septic/Distributive Shock
Wide pulse pressure — vasodilation drops DBP more than SBP (or SBP may even rise slightly with hyperdynamic CO) → pulse pressure widens.
Low/absent dicrotic notch — low SVR, vasoplegia.
Rapid diastolic decay/runoff — blood rapidly leaves the arterial tree due to low peripheral resistance.
PPV may be less reliable — variable vascular tone and often reduced compliance confound interpretation.
Core distinguishing principle: pulse pressure narrows in vasoconstricted states (haemorrhage) and widens in vasodilated states (sepsis) — because pulse pressure is primarily a function of stroke volume and arterial compliance/tone, not just SBP alone.

🎯 Examiner Pearls

Q: Underdamped vs overdamped trace — clinical consequence of each?
Underdamped (bubbles, excess tubing) → falsely elevated systolic reading, overshoot artifacts — risk of inappropriately treating a "hypertension" that isn't real. Overdamped (clot, kink, air) → falsely low systolic, narrowed pulse pressure, rounded waveform — risk of missing true hypertension or overestimating hypotension severity.
Q: Why does pulse pressure narrow in haemorrhagic shock but widen in septic shock?
Haemorrhage triggers compensatory vasoconstriction (↑SVR) which raises diastolic pressure relative to a falling systolic — narrowing the gap. Sepsis causes vasoplegia (↓SVR) which drops diastolic disproportionately, widening the gap — sometimes with a preserved or even elevated systolic from compensatory hyperdynamic cardiac output.
Q: What does the dicrotic notch actually represent?
Aortic valve closure — the brief pressure increase as retrograde flow closes the valve. Its height and position on the waveform reflect peripheral vascular resistance: high and close to the systolic peak in vasoconstricted states, low or nearly absent in vasodilated states.
