FAST / eFAST

Focused Assessment with Sonography for Trauma

📖 Definition

FAST = Focused Assessment with Sonography for Trauma. A rapid bedside ultrasound to detect free fluid (assumed to be blood in trauma) in the peritoneal cavity and pericardium. eFAST (extended) adds bilateral thoracic views for pneumothorax and haemothorax. It answers one question fast: is there free fluid where there shouldn't be?

🎯 Probe Positions

● 1 Subxiphoid (pericardium)
● 2 Right hepatorenal (Morison's pouch)
● 3 Left perisplenic
● 4 Suprapubic (pouch of Douglas)
● T Thoracic views — eFAST extension

🔍 The Four Core Views + Thoracic

1Subxiphoid / Pericardial
Looks for: Pericardial effusion, cardiac tamponade
Window: Probe under xiphisternum, angled up toward left shoulder — four-chamber cardiac view
Positive: Anechoic (black) stripe around the heart within the pericardium
2Right Hepatorenal — Morison's Pouch
Looks for: Free intraperitoneal blood
Window: Right mid-axillary line, between liver and right kidney
KEY: Most dependent part of peritoneal cavity in a supine patient — blood pools here FIRST. Highest-yield single view.
Positive: Anechoic stripe between liver and kidney (hepatorenal recess)
3Left Perisplenic
Looks for: Free blood between spleen and diaphragm / splenorenal recess
Window: Left posterior axillary line — "knuckles to the bed", probe more posterior and superior than the right
Positive: Anechoic stripe above spleen (subphrenic) or splenorenal space
4Suprapubic / Pelvic
Looks for: Free blood in most dependent pelvic recess
Window: Above pubic symphysis, bladder as acoustic window
Male: Rectovesical pouch  ·  Female: Rectouterine pouch of Douglas
Positive: Anechoic fluid posterior to bladder
TThoracic — eFAST Extension
Pneumothorax: Loss of lung sliding, absent comet-tail artefacts, "barcode/stratosphere sign" on M-mode (vs normal "seashore sign")
Haemothorax: Anechoic fluid above the diaphragm at the lung bases
Why it matters: Catches immediately life-threatening chest pathology basic FAST misses. Always mention eFAST in a viva.

🔀 FAST Result → Decision

Positive FAST — is the patient haemodynamically stable?
UNSTABLEStraight to theatre → laparotomy. NO CT — cannot safely scan a crashing patient.
STABLECT with contrast → characterise & grade injury, plan definitive management.
Negative FAST — but still unstable with abdominal mechanism?
DON'T TRUST ITNegative FAST does NOT exclude injury — retroperitoneal, hollow viscus, early bleed.
NEXTRepeat FAST, CT if stabilisable, or theatre. Check "blood on floor + 4 more".

⚠️ Limitations — Know These Cold

Can't identify fluid type
Blood vs urine vs ascites vs bowel content vs effusion — FAST shows fluid, not what it is.
Misses retroperitoneum
Pancreatic, duodenal, renal, aorta/IVC, pelvic fracture bleeds — the classic blind spot.
Needs ~200ml fluid
Small or early bleeds fall below detection threshold — hence false negatives.
Operator dependent
Sensitivity varies widely with skill and experience.
Poor for hollow viscus
Bowel & diaphragmatic injuries poorly detected — needs CT.
Can't grade injury
Positive tells you fluid is present, not the source or severity.
Image quality degraded by: obesity, subcutaneous emphysema, bowel gas, large body habitus. A technically difficult scan is not a negative scan.

🩸 "Blood on the Floor and Four More"

Five sites of fatal haemorrhage
On the floor — external: scalp, open fractures, wounds, scene loss
1. Chest — each hemithorax holds 1.5–2L (30–40% blood volume)
2. Abdomen — several litres; what FAST detects
3. Pelvis / retroperitoneum — 3–4L into a space FAST can't see
4. Long bones — closed femur 1–1.5L; bilateral femurs exsanguinate with clean sheets

🎯 Examiner Pearls

Q: Which single FAST view is highest yield and why?
Right hepatorenal (Morison's pouch) — it's the most dependent part of the peritoneal cavity in a supine patient, so free intraperitoneal blood collects there first. Highest sensitivity of the individual views.
Q: Positive FAST + unstable — do you CT first?
No. Unstable + positive FAST → theatre for laparotomy immediately. CT is for the stable patient to characterise injury. Never send a crashing patient to the scanner ("the doughnut of death").
Q: Negative FAST — does it exclude significant intraabdominal injury?
No. FAST needs ~200ml fluid, misses retroperitoneal and hollow viscus injury, and is operator dependent. A negative FAST in ongoing clinical suspicion warrants repeat scanning, CT, or laparotomy based on haemodynamics.
Q: How does eFAST detect pneumothorax?
Absence of lung sliding and comet-tail artefacts at the pleural line. On M-mode, normal lung shows the "seashore sign"; pneumothorax shows the "barcode" or "stratosphere sign". More sensitive than supine CXR for occult pneumothorax.
