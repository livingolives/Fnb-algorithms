🔬 Hypoxic Pulmonary Vasoconstriction (HPV)

HPV is an adaptive reflex that optimises V/Q matching. The sensor is pulmonary vascular smooth muscle responding to local alveolar PO₂ (PAO₂) — NOT arterial PaO₂. This distinction is frequently examined.
Collapsed lung (OLV)Alveolar PAO₂ falls → local hypoxia
→
HPV triggeredSmooth muscle contracts → pulmonary arteriole vasoconstriction in hypoxic segments
Blood redirectedAway from collapsed (hypoxic) segments → toward ventilated lung
→
V/Q matching improvedShunt fraction reduced · SpO₂ maintained during OLV

🚫 HPV Inhibitors — Know All of These

Volatile Anaesthetics
Halothane > isoflurane > sevoflurane/desflurane. Direct smooth muscle relaxation. Use TIVA (propofol infusion) for OLV when possible.
Calcium Channel Blockers
Block voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels in vascular smooth muscle → prevents HPV-mediated contraction.
Nitrates / GTN
NO-mediated vasodilation → blunts HPV response in hypoxic segments.
Beta-agonists
Salbutamol, adrenaline → β₂-mediated pulmonary vasodilation → HPV blunted.
Vasodilators
SNP, hydralazine, prostacyclin → non-selective pulmonary vasodilation → increases shunt.
High FiO₂ — partial exception
100% FiO₂ to ventilated lung can partially compensate for lost HPV by maximising O₂ in perfused lung.

🚨 SpO₂ Drops During OLV — Stepwise Management

1
FiO₂ to 100%
Immediate — maximises O₂ delivery to the ventilated dependent lung. Do this first, takes 10 seconds.
2
Check Tube Position — Fibreoptic Bronchoscopy
Most common correctable cause. DLT too deep → blocks RUL. Malpositioned blocker. Check before adding CPAP/PEEP — fixing position alone often resolves hypoxaemia.
3
Optimise Ventilated Lung — TV and RR
Ensure adequate minute ventilation. TV 6ml/kg IBW, adjust RR to maintain normocarbia. Avoid auto-PEEP from high RR.
4
CPAP 5–10 cmH₂O to Non-Operative (Collapsed) Lung
Maintains some alveolar recruitment → allows O₂ absorption without full ventilation. Does not disturb the surgical field significantly at low pressures. Reduces shunt fraction.
5
PEEP 5 cmH₂O to Dependent (Ventilated) Lung
Recruits collapsed alveoli in good lung → improves V/Q → reduces intrapulmonary shunt. Caution: excessive PEEP increases PVR → diverts blood back to non-ventilated lung — counterproductive.
6
Ask Surgeon — Partial Reinflation of Operative Lung
Intermittent reinflation (2-lung ventilation temporarily) is the correct surgical intervention — NOT ligation of pulmonary vessels. Accept surgical compromise if necessary to correct hypoxaemia.
7
Return to Two-Lung Ventilation
If all else fails — safety of patient supersedes surgical conditions. Fully reinflate, re-establish TLV, allow recovery before attempting OLV again.

🚑 Trauma-Specific Considerations

Trauma makes OLV harder — here's why
Blood in airways — haemothorax, pulmonary contusion. Secretions/blood in the collapsed lung will accumulate during OLV. DLT suction advantage here is critical.
Existing ETT — trauma patients often arrive intubated. Bronchial blocker avoids DLT exchange in C-spine injury, airway oedema, or difficult airway.
Pulmonary contusion — already impairs oxygenation before OLV starts. HPV may be blunted in contused lung. Expect worse baseline SpO₂ — plan earlier intervention thresholds.
TIVA preferred — avoid volatile agents to preserve HPV. Propofol infusion does not inhibit HPV. Use ketamine + propofol TIVA in haemodynamically compromised patients.
Ligate pulmonary vessel to operative lung for hypoxaemia → This is pneumonectomy, not an OLV hypoxaemia rescue. Ask for partial reinflation instead.

🎯 Examiner Pearls

Q: HPV is triggered by hypoxaemia — true or false?
False as stated. HPV is triggered by alveolar PAO₂ (local alveolar oxygen tension), not arterial PaO₂. The sensor is in the pulmonary vascular smooth muscle responding to local conditions. A patient can have normal arterial PaO₂ and still trigger HPV in a poorly ventilated segment.
Q: Why use TIVA rather than volatile agents for OLV?
Volatile agents inhibit HPV in a dose-dependent manner — halothane worst, sevoflurane/desflurane least. HPV is the primary defence against shunting during OLV. Inhibiting it increases intrapulmonary shunt → worsens hypoxaemia. Propofol TIVA does not inhibit HPV and is therefore preferred for thoracic OLV cases.
Q: Which side DLT in 95% of cases and why?
Left DLT. The right main bronchus is shorter (~2cm) and the right upper lobe bronchus arises almost immediately — a right DLT easily occludes the RUL. The longer left main bronchus (~5cm) provides more margin for correct positioning. Left DLT is used even for left-sided procedures in most cases.
Q: What is the most common correctable cause of hypoxaemia during OLV?
Malposition of the DLT or bronchial blocker — confirmed and corrected by fibreoptic bronchoscopy. This is why tube position check is step 2 in the hypoxaemia ladder, before adding CPAP/PEEP.
