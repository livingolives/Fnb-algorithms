Fascia Iliaca Block & LAST

Anatomy · Technique · Evidence · Local Anaesthetic Toxicity

🧬 Anatomy — Two Fascial Layers

Cross-Section — Anterior Thigh, Just Below Inguinal Ligament
Orientation: superficial→deep runs top to bottom; medial→lateral runs left to right (patient supine, viewer at foot of bed).

Needle passes skin → subcutaneous fat → fascia lata (POP 1) → small areolar gap → fascia iliaca (POP 2) → inject beneath fascia iliaca, superficial to iliacus muscle. LA spreads in this plane medially to reach the lumbar plexus branches.

Medial→lateral order of femoral triangle contents at the inguinal ligament: Vein, Artery, Nerve (mnemonic VAN). The femoral nerve sits lateral to the artery — inject lateral to the arterial pulse to stay clear of the vessels.

📊 Evidence-Based Benefits — Elderly Hip Fracture

Opioid-Sparing
Reduced systemic opioid requirement → less respiratory depression, less constipation, safer in frail elderly with reduced physiological reserve.
Reduced Delirium
Postoperative delirium is a major driver of morbidity/mortality in elderly hip fracture. Regional analgesia reduces incidence — a major reason it's guideline-endorsed.
Enables Positioning
A patient in agony cannot sit for spinal/epidural placement. Fascia iliaca block first makes neuraxial technique possible.
Guideline-Endorsed
AAGBI / NICE / National Hip Fracture Database (NHFD) recommend fascia iliaca block as part of the standard hip fracture pathway.

☠️ Local Anaesthetic Systemic Toxicity (LAST)

Maximum levobupivacaine dose: 2 mg/kg. Levobupivacaine and bupivacaine are highly lipophilic and cardiotoxic — bind avidly to cardiac Na⁺ channels — which is exactly why lipid emulsion rescue works so well for them specifically.

📈 Symptom Progression

Early — CNS
Perioral tingling, metallic taste, tinnitus, agitation, confusion
Progressing
Seizures → then CNS depression, coma
Cardiovascular — later, more dangerous
Arrhythmias, conduction block, cardiovascular collapse — bupivacaine binds cardiac Na channels avidly

🚨 LAST Management — Step by Step

1
Stop Injecting
Immediate — first action, before anything else.
2
Call for Help
Declare LAST explicitly. Get the lipid emulsion box.
3
Airway & Oxygenation
100% O₂, secure airway, ventilate. Avoid hypoxia, hypercapnia, acidosis — all worsen toxicity.
4
Seizure Control
Benzodiazepine first-line (midazolam). Avoid large-dose propofol — cardiac depressant, not a substitute for lipid emulsion.
5
Lipid Emulsion 20% — see dose box below
"Lipid sink" — sequesters lipophilic LA away from cardiac/neural tissue, provides fatty acid substrate, possible direct cardiotonic effect.
6
Modified ACLS for Arrhythmias
Avoid lidocaine (another Na-channel blocker) and vasopressin. Reduce adrenaline to <1 mcg/kg boluses (not standard 1mg). Amiodarone for VT. Prolonged CPR may be needed — lipid works, keep going.
7
Post-Event
Monitor minimum 6–12 hours — long-acting LA can cause recurrence. Report to LAST registry.
💉 Intralipid 20% — Exact Regimen (ASRA/AAGBI)
Bolus1.5 mL/kg over 1 minute
Infusion15 mL/kg/hr
If instability persistsRepeat bolus ×2 at 5-min intervals
If BP declinesDouble infusion to 30 mL/kg/hr
Maximum cumulative dose12 mL/kg
⚠ Don't Confuse Bolus Rate
Bolus is 1.5 mL/kg over 1 minute — a single timed bolus, NOT a continuous "per minute" infusion. Then switch to the separate 15 mL/kg/hr maintenance infusion. Getting this backwards is a common exam error.

🎯 Examiner Pearls

Q: Why does levobupivacaine cause more severe cardiotoxicity than lidocaine?
Bupivacaine/levobupivacaine bind cardiac Na⁺ channels with much higher affinity and dissociate much more slowly ("fast-in, slow-out" kinetics) — this is the basis of the Vaughan-Williams-related cardiotoxicity concept. Highly lipophilic, so also readily crosses into cardiac and neural tissue — exactly why lipid emulsion is so effective as an antidote.
Q: Why avoid vasopressin in LAST resuscitation?
Animal data suggests vasopressin worsens outcomes in LAST-associated cardiac arrest, possibly through excessive vasoconstriction and pulmonary haemorrhage. ASRA guidance specifically excludes it from the LAST resuscitation algorithm.
Q: Fascia lata vs fascia iliaca — which is more superficial?
Fascia lata is superficial (first pop) — the deep investing fascia of the whole thigh, continuous with the inguinal ligament, iliac crest, and pubic symphysis, and thickening laterally into the IT band. Fascia iliaca is deeper (second pop) — covers iliacus and psoas, with the lumbar plexus nerve branches running beneath it.
