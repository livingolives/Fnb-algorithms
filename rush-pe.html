📖 RUSH — Rapid Ultrasound for SHock

Not "Rapid Ultrasound for Haemorrhage" — a common exam error. RUSH is a general bedside ultrasound protocol to rapidly differentiate the type of undifferentiated shock: hypovolaemic, cardiogenic, obstructive, or distributive.

Think of the obstruction as a clamp on cardiac output: everything behind it backs up (tank distended), everything in front of it is starved (pipes collapsed) — same peripheral vasoconstriction physiology as hypovolaemia, but from a mechanical block rather than true volume loss. IVC is the discriminator: collapsed in hypovolaemia, distended in obstruction.

🚨 Obstructive Shock — Three Causes RUSH Differentiates

🫁Tension Pneumothorax
Absent lung sliding
M-mode: barcode / stratosphere sign (vs normal seashore sign)
🫀Cardiac Tamponade
Pericardial effusion visualised
Diastolic RV collapse — external pressure compresses thin-walled RV when its pressure is lowest
Distended neck veins, distended IVC
🔴Abdominal Compartment Syndrome
Not a direct visual sign like the other two — diagnosed primarily by IAP measurement
Ultrasound supports via VEXUS scoring — hepatic/portal vein Doppler changes as a consequence of raised IAP

🫀 RUSH Findings — Massive PE

RV Dilatation
RV:LV ratio >1 — pressure overload from clot obstructing outflow. NOT collapse — that's the tamponade sign.
D-Shaped LV
Interventricular septum flattens/bows into LV from RV pressure overload — makes LV cross-section look "D" shaped. It's the septum causing this, not the RV itself.
McConnell's Sign
RV free wall akinesia with preserved apical contraction — specific sign for acute PE, distinguishes from chronic RV strain.
IVC Distended
Back-pressure from obstructed RV outflow — classic obstructive "tank" pattern.
Pipes — Femoral Vein Non-Compressible
DVT source identified at the groin — same probe position doubles as embolic source screen while assessing "pipes."
⚠ Don't Confuse Obstructive Causes
RV diastolic collapse in massive PE → That's tamponade. PE causes RV dilatation/strain from internal pressure overload, not external compression.

⚖️ The Trauma-Specific Thrombolysis Dilemma

Recent major surgery/trauma (within 10 days, some sources cite up to 3 weeks) is a RELATIVE, not absolute, contraindication to systemic thrombolysis. Day 4 post-op sits well within this window.
The actual dilemma: untreated massive PE with shock carries very high mortality. Relative contraindications CAN be overridden in a life-threatening massive PE — it becomes a genuine risk-benefit judgement made under time pressure, not simply deferred to committee. Absolute contraindications cannot be overridden.

🎯 Management if Thrombolysis Avoided

1
Anticoagulation Alone
Unfractionated heparin infusion — preferred in unstable/high-bleeding-risk patients as short-acting and reversible with protamine if bleeding occurs or urgent surgery needed.
2
Catheter-Directed Therapy
Catheter-directed thrombolysis (CDT) — low-dose lysis delivered directly into the clot via IR, significantly reduced bleeding risk vs systemic lysis. Often the answer when systemic lysis too risky but clot burden reduction still needed.
Mechanical thrombectomy — aspiration devices (FlowTriever, Penumbra), no lytic drug at all.
3
Surgical Embolectomy
For patients failing/unsuitable for the above, particularly with absolute contraindication to any lytic therapy and cardiac surgery available.
4
VA-ECMO — Bridge Therapy
Refractory shock — buys time for definitive clot management by IR/surgery.
+
Supportive Care Throughout
Careful fluid management — RV already strained, avoid volume overload worsening it. Noradrenaline preferred over aggressive fluids to support RV coronary perfusion pressure.

🎯 Examiner Pearls

Q: What does RUSH actually stand for?
Rapid Ultrasound for SHock — not haemorrhage. A general shock differentiation protocol assessing Pump (heart), Tank (IVC), and Pipes (aorta + femoral vein).
Q: Why is IVC distended but the aorta collapsed in obstructive shock?
The obstruction acts as a clamp on cardiac output. Venous blood dams up behind the block (IVC distended), while the arterial side receives critically reduced forward flow, triggering compensatory vasoconstriction (aorta/pipes collapsed) — same peripheral shutdown physiology as hypovolaemia, different cause.
Q: RV diastolic collapse vs RV dilatation — which condition, which sign?
Tamponade → RV diastolic collapse (external compression from pericardial fluid). Massive PE → RV dilatation/strain, D-shaped LV, McConnell's sign (internal pressure overload from obstructed outflow). Do not confuse these two obstructive causes.
Q: Can you thrombolyse a massive PE patient 4 days post-laparotomy?
Recent surgery within 10 days is a relative, not absolute, contraindication. In a life-threatening massive PE with shock, the mortality of untreated PE may outweigh the bleeding risk — this becomes a genuine risk-benefit decision, often favouring catheter-directed therapy or even systemic lysis over withholding reperfusion entirely.
