🔄 Damage Control Surgery — Three Phases

Definition: A staged surgical strategy in physiologically compromised polytrauma — abbreviated initial surgery to control haemorrhage and contamination, followed by ICU resuscitation to correct the lethal triad, then planned definitive surgery once physiology is restored.
I
DCL — Control
Haemorrhage control (packing, vascular clamping). Contamination control (bowel stapling, not anastomosis). Temporary abdominal closure (VAC/Bogota bag).
TARGET <60 MIN
II
ICU — Resuscitate
Correct lethal triad. MTP completion. Warming. Ventilate. Optimise coagulation. Target physiological normalisation.
24–72 HOURS
III
Relook — Definitive
Unpack, reassess. Definitive repair — bowel anastomosis, vascular reconstruction. Fascial closure if possible.
WHEN CRITERIA MET

💉 Anaesthetic Induction — Shocked Patient

RSI is mandatory — full stomach assumed in all trauma. Aspiration risk. Video laryngoscopy (C-MAC/Glidescope) as first choice — not backup. Pre-oxygenate with BVM if SpO₂ dropping. Minimise apnoeic time.
Ketamine
1–2 mg/kg IV
Use: First choice in haemodynamic compromise.

Sympathomimetic → ↑HR, ↑BP via catecholamine release.
Analgesia + anaesthesia in one agent.
Preserves airway reflexes at sub-anaesthetic doses.
Bronchodilation — useful if reactive airways.
Etomidate
0.2–0.3 mg/kg IV
Caution: Adrenocortical suppression after single dose — suppresses cortisol for 24–48h. Significant in shock patients with relative adrenal insufficiency. Many trauma centres avoid. Use only if ketamine contraindicated.
Rocuronium
1.2 mg/kg IV
Use: Preferred NMBD for RSI in trauma. High dose achieves RSI conditions equivalent to suxamethonium within 60 seconds.

Reversible with sugammadex 16 mg/kg if cannot intubate/ventilate.
Suxamethonium
1.5 mg/kg IV
Caution: Use with awareness — hyperkalaemia risk if denervation injury (SCI, burns, crush). In pure haemorrhagic shock without these — still acceptable if rocuronium unavailable. Cannot be reversed.
Drug dose reductions in shock: Induction agents at 30–50% of standard dose — reduced cardiac output means slower circulation time and higher brain concentration per dose. A shocked patient on 80mg/kg ketamine will behave like a normal patient on 200mg/kg. Titrate carefully — haemodynamic collapse on induction is survivable; awareness is not ideal but preferable to cardiac arrest.

The most important trigger: "The patient is dying on the table." When the anaesthetist recognises physiological irreversibility and the surgeon cannot achieve haemostasis — pack, close, and go to ICU. A living patient with packed abdomen is better than a dead patient with a perfect anastomosis.

Aim for definitive surgery at 48 hours ideally — earlier if physiology restored quickly, later if still correcting. Beyond 72 hours without fascial closure — risk of loss of fascial domain, fistula formation, and abdominal wall reconstruction increases significantly.

⚠️ Examiner Traps

Don't fall for these
Maintain MAP >65 throughout → Permissive hypotension MAP 50–65 until haemorrhage controlled — higher MAP drives more bleeding.
Target Hb >10 g/dL intraoperatively → >8 g/dL for young patient without cardiac disease or TBI.
Target pH >7.35 intraoperatively → >7.2 is the working target during active haemorrhage — 7.35 is the ICU target post-DCL.
Etomidate safe in all trauma patients → Single dose suppresses adrenocortical axis 24–48h. Avoid in shocked patients with relative adrenal insufficiency — use ketamine instead.
Permissive hypotension in TBI + haemorrhage → MAP >80 if TBI coexists. CPP cannot be compromised. Simultaneous TBI + haemorrhage is the most physiologically challenging scenario in trauma anaesthesia.

🎯 Examiner Pearls

Q: Why reduce induction agent dose in shocked patients?
Reduced cardiac output → slower circulation time → drug reaches the brain in higher effective concentration per administered dose. Additionally, the blood-brain barrier may be disrupted. Standard doses cause profound cardiovascular depression. Rule: give 30–50% of normal dose, titrate to effect, have vasopressor drawn up before induction.
Q: TBI + haemorrhagic shock — what is the single most difficult management conflict?
Permissive hypotension (standard for haemorrhagic shock) vs mandatory normotension/hypertension (required for CPP maintenance in TBI). Resolution: maintain MAP >80 throughout — accept higher transfusion requirement and surgical haemorrhage to protect the brain. TBI mortality from cerebral ischaemia outweighs marginal increase in haemorrhage from higher MAP in most cases.
Q: What does "damage control" mean — and where did the concept originate?
The term originates from the US Navy — damage control is the emergency procedure to keep a ship afloat after catastrophic damage, prioritising survival over perfection. Rotondo et al. (J Trauma 1993) applied the concept to surgery: abbreviated initial procedure to prevent death from physiological exhaustion, accepting an incomplete repair in exchange for a living patient.
Q: What is the role of tranexamic acid in emergency laparotomy?
Give within 3 hours of injury (CRASH-2 trial). 1g IV over 10 min loading, then 1g over 8 hours. Inhibits fibrinolysis — prevents clot breakdown. After 3 hours, TXA may be harmful (promotes pathological clotting without benefit). Do not wait to give — if in doubt and within window, give immediately.
