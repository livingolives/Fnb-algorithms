Junctional Haemorrhage

Surface Zones · Junctional Tourniquets · When Standard TQ Fails

📖 Definition

Junctional haemorrhage = bleeding at anatomical junctions between the torso and limbs/head — locations where standard limb tourniquets cannot be applied effectively because the vessels are too proximal, too deep, or there's no complete bony ring to compress against.

⚠️ Two Different Zone Systems — Don't Confuse Them

Common Exam Error
Junctional zones (this page) = external, surface anatomical locations — groin, axilla, neck. Where you apply an external compression device.
REBOA aortic zones (Zone I/II/III — see REBOA page) = internal, intravascular locations inside the aorta itself, defined by which branch vessels sit above/below the balloon. Where you place a catheter.
These are completely different classification systems for completely different interventions. Stating REBOA's Zone I-III when asked about junctional anatomical zones is a guaranteed mark loss.

🎯 The Three Junctional Zones (Surface Anatomy)

🦵Zone 1 — Groin / Inguinal
Vessels: Femoral artery and vein
Why standard TQ fails: too proximal — no distal bony ring below the injury to compress against once above mid-thigh
💪Zone 2 — Axilla
Vessels: Axillary artery and vein
Why standard TQ fails: vessels run against the ribcage/chest wall — no limb bone to compress against at this level
🧠Zone 3 — Neck
Vessels: Carotid, subclavian at base of neck
Why standard TQ fails: circumferential neck compression would occlude the airway — impossible to apply a conventional tourniquet
Some classifications add a pelvic/perineal junction as a fourth zone.

🔧 Why Standard Limb Tourniquets Don't Work Here

Limb Tourniquet
Circumferential compression
Compresses soft tissue against a complete bony ring
No major body cavity behind the compression point
Reliable, reproducible occlusion
Junctional Zone
No complete bony ring available
Vessels run close to major body cavities (pelvis, thorax, abdomen)
Relies on focal compression against a specific bony landmark only
Less reliable, harder to apply under time pressure
This limitation is exactly why REBOA emerged as the preferred solution for proximal junctional/truncal haemorrhage that external devices struggle to control reliably.

🔗 When External Devices Fail — REBOA

For groin/pelvic junctional haemorrhage uncontrolled by external junctional tourniquets, REBOA Zone III (below the lowest renal artery, above the aortic bifurcation) is the escalation — occludes the distal aorta supplying the pelvis and lower limbs while preserving renal, mesenteric, and upper limb perfusion.

Full REBOA zone anatomy, technique, and the REBOA vs EDT decision ladder are covered on the dedicated REBOA page — this page focuses specifically on the external/surface junctional zones and devices that precede that escalation.

🎯 Examiner Pearls

Q: What makes a haemorrhage "junctional" rather than just proximal limb bleeding?
The absence of a complete bony ring to compress against — groin, axilla, and neck all have vessels running close to major body cavities without the circumferential bony support a standard limb tourniquet relies on.
Q: Junctional zones vs REBOA zones — how do you keep them separate in a viva?
Junctional zones are external, surface anatomical locations (groin/axilla/neck) for compression device placement. REBOA zones are internal, intravascular aortic segments (I/II/III) defined by branch vessel anatomy, for balloon catheter placement. Different classification systems for different interventions — never conflate them.
Q: Why can't you just apply a tourniquet to the neck for a junctional carotid injury?
Circumferential neck compression would occlude the airway before achieving vascular control — anatomically and physiologically impossible to apply a conventional tourniquet mechanism at this junction.
