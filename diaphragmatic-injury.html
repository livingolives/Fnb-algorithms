Traumatic Diaphragmatic Injury

Left vs Right · CT Signs · Delayed Presentation

⚖️ Left vs Right — Which Side, and Why

Left Side
More commonly diagnosed — no solid organ buffer. Stomach/bowel herniate visibly into thorax, easy to spot on imaging (gastric bubble in chest, NG tube coiled above diaphragm).
Right Side
Liver buffers/protects — solid bulky organ often doesn't herniate as obviously, or the finding just looks like an elevated hemidiaphragm rather than a foreign structure in the chest. Likely under-diagnosed, not necessarily less common as an injury.
Precision point: say "left-sided injury is more commonly recognised/diagnosed" — not definitively "more common." True incidence may be similar; the diagnostic visibility differs due to what's herniating.

📷 Imaging Signs

NG/Ryles Tube in the Thorax
Most specific and easiest-to-spot sign — tube visibly coils or terminates above the diaphragm on CXR instead of in the expected abdominal position.
Irregular Diaphragmatic Contour
Loss of the normal smooth diaphragmatic line — indistinct or interrupted border on CXR/CT.
Focal Diaphragmatic Discontinuity
Visible gap in the diaphragmatic muscle on CT — direct visualisation of the defect itself.
Unexplained Mediastinal Shift
Contralateral shift without an obvious pneumothorax explaining it — should raise suspicion for mass effect from herniated abdominal contents.

🎯 Collar Sign & Dependent Viscera Sign

Collar Sign — Axial CT Cross-Section (Left Chest)
Simplified axial CT-style cross-section. Herniated stomach passes through the diaphragmatic defect and is pinched into an hourglass/collar shape at the level of the tear — the classic collar sign. The left lung is visibly compressed by the herniated viscus.
Dependent Viscera Sign — Axial CT Comparison (Supine Patient)
True axial (transverse) CT slice — the plane this sign is actually evaluated on. Patient supine. Normal: intact diaphragm holds the stomach anteriorly/medially — a visible fat plane or gap separates it from the posterior chest wall. Ruptured: with no diaphragmatic support, gravity pulls the stomach straight down (posteriorly, since the patient is supine) until it rests directly against the posterior ribs/vertebra, obliterating that normal fat plane.

⏱ Why Herniation Is Often Delayed

⚠ Common Misconception
The lung physically blocks/stops the organ from herniating → Herniation is driven by the pleuroperitoneal PRESSURE GRADIENT, not lung tissue acting as a barrier
At injury: small tear may not immediately herniate if intra-abdominal pressure isn't significantly higher than intrathoracic pressure — tear sits sealed by adjacent organs/omentum
↓ time passes — months to years
Later event raises intra-abdominal pressure: straining, coughing, ileus/bowel distension, pregnancy, abdominal surgery
↓
Pressure gradient tips: intra-abdominal pressure now exceeds intrathoracic pressure sufficiently
↓
Negative intrathoracic pressure of normal inspiration ACTIVELY SUCKS abdominal contents through the defect — this is the true driving force
This mechanism is exactly why diaphragmatic injuries can present months to years after the original trauma — a missed small tear sits quiescent until a later physiological event tips the pressure balance and pulls viscera through.

🚨 Consequences If Missed

Respiratory: herniated viscera compress lung → V/Q mismatch, hypoxia, atelectasis/consolidation.

Strangulation risk: the defect can act like a hernia neck — herniated bowel can become incarcerated or strangulated, leading to bowel ischaemia/perforation within the chest — a surgical emergency with high mortality if not recognised.

Aspiration risk: gastric herniation with obstruction increases aspiration risk.

🎯 Examiner Pearls

Q: Why is right-sided diaphragmatic injury harder to diagnose than left?
The liver buffers the right side — it's a solid, bulky organ that doesn't produce the obvious "bowel/stomach in the chest" appearance seen with left-sided herniation. Right-sided injury may just look like an elevated hemidiaphragm, easily missed or misattributed.
Q: What actually drives delayed herniation — is it the lung releasing its "hold" on the organ?
No — the lung does not act as a barrier. Herniation is governed by the pleuroperitoneal pressure gradient. A later rise in intra-abdominal pressure (straining, ileus, pregnancy) combined with the negative intrathoracic pressure generated during normal inspiration actively draws abdominal contents through the defect over time.
Q: Collar sign vs dependent viscera sign — what's the difference?
Collar sign describes the SHAPE of the herniated viscus — pinched/hourglass at the level of the defect. Dependent viscera sign describes the POSITION of the organ — resting against the posterior chest wall due to loss of diaphragmatic support, rather than its normal anterior position. Different aspects of the same underlying pathology.
