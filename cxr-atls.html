Trauma Chest X-Ray Interpretation

Systematic Approach · ATLS 10th Edition
Trauma CXR is almost always a supine AP portable film — different appearance and limitations compared to an erect PA film. Mediastinal structures appear magnified, small pneumothoraces and effusions are harder to detect (fluid/air layers out posteriorly rather than forming an obvious meniscus/apical line), and rotation is common. Always interpret with this context in mind.

📋 Systematic Approach

1
Adequacy & Rotation
Check patient ID, film quality/penetration. Assess rotation — medial ends of clavicles should be equidistant from the spinous processes. Rotation distorts mediastinal width assessment.
2
Airway & Tubes/Lines
Trachea midline or deviated? ETT tip position, NG tube course, central line tip position — see table below.
3
Mediastinum
Widened mediastinum (>8cm at aortic knob on supine AP, or mediastinal:chest width ratio >0.25) → suspect traumatic aortic injury — needs CT angiogram. Also check for pneumomediastinum (Macklin effect).
4
Pleural Spaces — Pneumothorax
Look for visceral pleural line, absent lung markings peripherally. On supine film, air rises anteromedially/subpulmonic — look for deep sulcus sign (abnormally deep, lucent costophrenic angle) rather than a classic apical rim.
5
Pleural Spaces — Haemothorax
On supine film, blood layers out posteriorly first — produces diffuse hazy opacification of the hemithorax rather than an obvious meniscus. Can mask/mimic a lung contusion. Up to 1L can be present before an obvious meniscus appears on supine film.
6
Lung Fields
Contusion (patchy, non-segmental opacity, doesn't respect fissures — distinguishes from pneumonia which does), collapse, foreign bodies.
7
Bones
Rib fractures (note number/location — first/second rib fracture suggests high-energy trauma, associated great vessel injury), clavicle, scapula, sternum (best seen on lateral).
8
Diaphragm & Subdiaphragmatic
Elevated hemidiaphragm, NG tube coiling above diaphragm (diaphragmatic rupture — see dedicated page), free air under diaphragm (hollow viscus perforation).
9
Soft Tissues
Subcutaneous emphysema (surgical emphysema) — tracking air in soft tissue planes, suggests pneumothorax, tracheobronchial injury, or oesophageal rupture.

🎯 Deep Sulcus Sign — Supine Pneumothorax

Supine AP Chest — Right Deep Sulcus Sign
On a supine film, free air doesn't rise to the apex like on an erect film — it can collect in two distinct locations, producing two different signs. Anteromedial air outlines the cardiac border/mediastinum (sharp pericardial edge, medial lucent stripe) — a separate finding. The deep sulcus sign specifically results from air tracking into the lateral and subpulmonic costophrenic recess, which runs obliquely downward and backward — as air collects here it pushes the diaphragm down laterally, producing the abnormally deep, lucent "V" seen on the affected side, rather than the classic apical rim seen erect. Easily missed if you're only looking for the "textbook" erect-film appearance.

⚠️ Widened Mediastinum — Aortic Injury

>8cm at the level of the aortic knob on a supine AP film (or mediastinal-width:chest-width ratio >0.25) → suspicious for traumatic aortic injury (usually at the ligamentum arteriosum/isthmus).

Other CXR signs supporting aortic injury:
Apical Pleural Cap
Blood tracking over the lung apex — left-sided cap most specific.
Depressed Left Main Bronchus
Displaced >40° below horizontal by mediastinal haematoma.
NG Tube Deviation
Oesophagus/NG tube pushed to the right by haematoma.
Loss of Aortopulmonary Window
Normally clear space obliterated by haematoma.
CXR is a screening tool only — any suspicious finding mandates CT angiogram for definitive diagnosis. A normal mediastinal width does NOT exclude aortic injury reliably enough to skip CT if clinical suspicion is high (deceleration mechanism).

🔧 Tubes & Lines — Expected Position

Device
Correct Position
ETT tip
3–5cm above carina — too low risks right main bronchus intubation
NG/OG tube
Tip below diaphragm, in stomach. Coiling ABOVE diaphragm = diaphragmatic rupture
Central line tip
At cavoatrial junction — SVC, not in the right atrium
Chest drain
All side holes within pleural cavity — check radiopaque line marker
Trachea
Midline. Deviation AWAY from a pneumothorax suggests tension; deviation TOWARDS suggests collapse
⚠ Examiner Traps
Tension pneumothorax is a CLINICAL diagnosis — do NOT wait for CXR to treat it. Needle/finger thoracostomy first if clinically suspected (tracheal shift, absent breath sounds, haemodynamic compromise), image only confirms afterward or in the stable patient.
Supine film haemothorax can layer out as diffuse haziness mimicking contusion — up to 1L of blood may not show an obvious meniscus.
Rib fracture numbers matter — 1st/2nd rib fracture implies high-energy transfer, raises suspicion for great vessel/brachial plexus injury even without direct visualisation of vascular injury.

🎯 Examiner Pearls

Q: Why does a pneumothorax look different on a supine trauma CXR compared to an erect film?
On an erect film, free air rises to the apex, producing the classic visible pleural line/rim. Supine, air can collect anteromedially (outlining the cardiac border) or in the lateral/subpulmonic costophrenic recess — it's specifically this second location, as air pushes the obliquely-angled diaphragm down laterally, that produces the deep sulcus sign (abnormally lucent, deep costophrenic angle) rather than an apical rim. Missing this appearance is a common source of missed pneumothorax on trauma CXR.
Q: What mediastinal width on supine CXR raises suspicion for traumatic aortic injury, and what's your next step?
>8cm at the aortic knob, or mediastinal:chest width ratio >0.25. CXR is only a screening tool — any suspicious finding, or high clinical suspicion from mechanism alone, mandates CT angiogram for definitive diagnosis. A normal CXR does not reliably exclude aortic injury.
Q: Should you wait for a CXR before treating suspected tension pneumothorax?
No. Tension pneumothorax is a clinical diagnosis — tracheal deviation, absent breath sounds, hyperresonance, haemodynamic compromise. Treat immediately with needle or finger thoracostomy; imaging only confirms afterward or is used when the diagnosis is less clear in a stable patient.
