🔬 Why Henderson-Hasselbalch Falls Short

Henderson-Hasselbalch (pH = pKa + log[HCO₃⁻/CO₂]) treats HCO₃⁻ as a cause of pH. It isn't. It's a dependent variable — it settles wherever charge balance demands. HH describes the relationship mathematically but implies a causality that doesn't exist, and cannot explain hyperchloraemic acidosis or hypoalbuminaemic alkalosis.

⚙️ The Three Independent Variables

Stewart (1981) asked: what actually determines pH? His answer: three and only three independent variables. Everything else — H⁺, HCO₃⁻, OH⁻ — is dependent and falls out of solving the charge balance equation.

Stewart Charge Balance
SID + (A⁻) − (HCO₃⁻ + CO₃²⁻ + OH⁻ − H⁺) = 0
Electroneutrality is non-negotiable — plasma charge must always balance.
SID = net strong ion charge (fixed). A⁻ = weak acid dissociation (albumin, phosphate). HCO₃⁻ adjusts passively to satisfy the equation. It cannot do otherwise.

⚡ Strong Ion Difference — Simplified

SID (simplified) = Na⁺ − Cl⁻

Normal plasma: Na⁺ ~140, Cl⁻ ~102 → SID ~38–40 mEq/L.

0.9% saline: Na⁺ 154, Cl⁻ 154 → SID = 0. Pour it in, Cl⁻ rises disproportionately relative to Na⁺ → SID narrows → bicarbonate falls to maintain charge balance → hyperchloraemic metabolic acidosis.

Vomiting: Cl⁻ lost in gastric acid → SID widens → HCO₃⁻ rises → hypochloraemic metabolic alkalosis. Same mechanism, opposite direction.

🧪 Atot & Albumin — The Hidden Variable

Albumin carries ~12 mEq/L net negative charge at physiological pH. It is a significant contributor to plasma electroneutrality — but invisible to Henderson-Hasselbalch.

When albumin falls → fewer anions from Atot → the charge gap left by missing albumin must be filled by rising HCO₃⁻ → metabolic alkalosis. No acid lost. No base gained. Pure charge accounting.

Clinical trap: hypoalbuminaemic alkalosis can mask a genuine coexisting acidosis — the pH looks less severe than it truly is. Albumin infusion in ICU will slightly drop pH by restoring Atot — not because it's harmful, but because it's revealing the true acid-base picture underneath.

🔢 Corrected Anion Gap

Standard AG = Na⁺ − (Cl⁻ + HCO₃⁻). Normal ~12 mEq/L — mostly albumin's negative charge sitting unaccounted for in the formula.

If albumin is low, the AG shrinks artificially — unmeasured anions (lactate, ketones, uraemic acids) hide beneath a "normal" number.
Figge Correction Formula
Corrected AG = Measured AG + 0.25 × (40 − albumin g/L)
Rule of thumb: for every 4 g/L albumin falls below 40, add 1 mEq/L to the AG.
Albumin 20 g/L → add 5 mEq/L. A "normal" AG of 12 becomes 17 — high AG acidosis unmasked.

📋 Stewart-Based ABG Interpretation — Step by Step

1
pH — direction of primary disturbance
Normal 7.35–7.45. Acidaemia <7.35. Alkalaemia >7.45. What direction is the problem?
2
PCO₂ — respiratory component
Normal 35–45. If acidaemia + high PCO₂ → respiratory acidosis. If acidaemia + low PCO₂ → respiratory compensation for metabolic acidosis (or primary resp alkalosis with metabolic acidosis).
3
SID — is there a chloride problem?
Simplified SID = Na⁺ − Cl⁻. Normal ~38–40.
SID < 38 → hyperchloraemic acidosis component
SID > 42 → hypochloraemic alkalosis component
4
Atot — correct for albumin
For every 4 g/L albumin below 40 → expect 0.1 unit rise in pH (alkalosis contribution). Low albumin masks coexisting acidosis and artificially narrows the AG.
5
Corrected AG — unmeasured anions
Apply Figge correction. Corrected AG >12 → unmeasured anions present (lactate, ketones, uraemic acids, toxins). Cross-check with measured lactate.
6
Synthesise — decompose the base deficit
Using Fencl-Stewart approach, total BD is the sum of:
BD(Cl⁻) = chloride effect from SID narrowing
BD(Alb) = albumin effect (usually alkalosis — negative BD contribution)
BD(UA) = unmeasured anion effect (corrected AG − 12)
These should add up to the measured BD.

Step 2 — PCO₂: 38 → normal/slightly low → not respiratory cause. Metabolic acidosis with mild respiratory compensation.
Step 3 — SID: Na⁺ 138 − Cl⁻ 114 = 24 mEq/L ↓↓ LOW
Normal is ~40. SID severely narrowed → hyperchloraemic acidosis is the primary driver.
Step 4 — Albumin effect: Albumin 22 g/L (normal 40). Deficit = 18 g/L → alkalosis contribution of ~0.45 pH units being masked. MASKS ALKALOSIS
True acidosis is worse than pH 7.28 suggests.
Step 5 — Corrected AG: Measured AG = 138 − (114 + 17) = 7.
Corrected AG = 7 + 0.25 × 18 = 11.5 ≈ 12 NORMAL
No significant unmeasured anions. Lactate 1.1 confirms this. No occult lactic acidosis.
Stewart Interpretation
Hyperchloraemic normal-AG metabolic acidosis — caused by excessive 0.9% saline narrowing SID to 24.

Hypoalbuminaemia (22 g/L) is generating a concurrent masked alkalosis — the real pH is being partially defended by this effect; without it, pH would be lower.

Management: Switch to balanced crystalloid (RL or Plasma-Lyte). Stop saline. Address hypoalbuminaemia. Do NOT give bicarbonate — treats the number without fixing the SID. Monitor serial Cl⁻ and SID as resuscitation endpoint, not just pH and HCO₃⁻.

⚠️ Examiner Traps

Do not fall for these
HCO₃⁻ is low therefore metabolic acidosis — cause unclear → Ask why. Check SID, albumin, corrected AG before concluding.
Normal AG — no unmeasured anions → Only safe if albumin is normal. Always correct the AG for albumin first.
Give bicarbonate for metabolic acidosis → Not for hyperchloraemic acidosis — fix the SID with balanced crystalloid. Bicarb generates CO₂, risks intracellular acidosis.
Albumin infusion improves acid-base → It restores Atot — may slightly drop pH by unmasking the true picture. Not a treatment for acidosis.

🎯 Examiner Pearls

Q: What are Stewart's three independent variables?
PCO₂ (set by ventilation), SID (set by kidneys and fluids given), and Atot (total weak acid concentration — mainly albumin + phosphate). Everything else including pH and HCO₃⁻ is dependent.
Q: Why does 0.9% saline cause acidosis?
Saline SID = 0. Infusing it dilutes plasma toward zero-SID composition — Cl⁻ rises disproportionately relative to Na⁺, narrowing plasma SID. Charge balance demands HCO₃⁻ falls. Normal anion gap, hyperchloraemic metabolic acidosis.
Q: Patient has AG of 10 and albumin of 18 g/L. Is the AG truly normal?
No. Corrected AG = 10 + 0.25 × (40−18) = 10 + 5.5 = 15.5 — elevated. There are unmeasured anions hiding behind the low albumin. Always correct before concluding.
Q: Flaw of Henderson-Hasselbalch in one sentence?
It treats HCO₃⁻ as an independent determinant of pH when it is actually a dependent variable — a consequence of SID, Atot, and PCO₂, not a cause.
