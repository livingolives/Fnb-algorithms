Burns Resuscitation

Parkland Formula · Rule of Nines · ABA Guidelines · Escharotomy

📐 TBSA Assessment

WALLACE RULE OF NINES — ADULTS
PAEDIATRIC (Lund-Browder chart): Head = 18% at birth (decreases with age). Each leg = 14% at birth (increases with age). Use Lund-Browder — Rule of Nines INACCURATE in children.
What counts as burn for TBSA: Partial thickness (superficial + deep) + full thickness. Exclude superficial/erythema — sunburn-like, no blistering, heals within 7 days.

Palm method: Patient's palm (including fingers) = approximately 1% TBSA. Useful for scattered/irregular burns.

💧 Parkland Formula — Fluid Resuscitation

4 ml × kg × % TBSA
Fluid of choice: Hartmann's solution (Lactated Ringer's)
Give over 24 hours from time of burn (not time of arrival)

First 8 hours: Give ½ of total volume
Next 16 hours: Give remaining ½

Example: 80kg, 40% TBSA
4 × 80 × 40 = 12,800 ml
First 8h: 6,400ml · Next 16h: 6,400ml
⚠ COMMON MISTAKES:
· Second half over 16 hours NOT 18 hours (8 + 16 = 24 total)
· Clock starts from TIME OF BURN — not arrival. Subtract pre-hospital time.
· Do NOT include maintenance fluids in Parkland calculation — add separately
· Do NOT use colloid in first 24 hours (glycocalyx disrupted — colloid leaks out)
· Parkland is a starting point — titrate to urine output

📊 Monitoring Targets

URINE OUTPUT
Adults: 0.5–1 ml/kg/hr
Children: 1 ml/kg/hr
Electrical burns/rhabdo: 1–2 ml/kg/hr
Best guide to resuscitation adequacy
LACTATE / BE
Lactate <2 mmol/L
Base excess >-2
Rising lactate = under-resuscitation or sepsis
CLINICAL
HR <110 bpm
MAP 65–70 mmHg
Capillary refill <2 sec
Mental status
TEMPERATURE
Burns patients lose heat rapidly — large surface area + fluid evaporation
Target: >36.5°C
Warm fluids, warm environment
Over-resuscitation = "fluid creep" — too much fluid → abdominal compartment syndrome, pulmonary oedema, extremity compartment syndrome, orbital compartment syndrome. Titrate DOWN if urine output >1ml/kg/hr consistently.

💨 Inhalation Injury

Triad: Burns in enclosed space · Carbonaceous sputum · Singed nasal hairs/eyebrows

Mechanism:
· Supraglottic: Heat injury → rapid oedema → airway obstruction. Intubate EARLY — airway can close within hours.
· Infraglottic: Chemical injury from toxic gases → mucosal damage → bronchospasm → ARDS
· Systemic: CO poisoning · Cyanide poisoning

Inhalation injury ↑ fluid resuscitation requirements by 30–50% above Parkland estimate.

Airway: Early intubation if stridor, hoarseness, facial burns, oedema. Nasotracheal if oral burns severe. Surgical airway if oedema too severe.

🫁 Carbon Monoxide Poisoning

Mechanism: CO binds Hb with 250× affinity of O₂ → carboxyhaemoglobin (COHb) → ↓ O₂ carrying capacity + leftward shift of O₂ dissociation curve → tissue hypoxia

SpO₂ is FALSELY NORMAL — pulse oximeter cannot distinguish COHb from OxyHb. Use co-oximetry (ABG).

Symptoms by COHb level:
· <10%: Headache, nausea
· 10–30%: Confusion, weakness
· 30–50%: Syncope, seizures
· >50%: Coma, death

Treatment: 100% O₂ via NRB mask (half-life COHb: 5h room air → 90 min on 100% O₂)
Hyperbaric O₂ (HBO): COHb >25% · Pregnancy · Neurological symptoms · COHb >15% in children

🔪 Escharotomy

Indication: Circumferential full thickness burns → eschar is non-elastic → compartment syndrome as oedema develops

Sites:
· Limbs: Medial and lateral longitudinal incisions through eschar
· Chest: Bilateral anterior axillary line incisions + transverse subcostal connecting incision → allows chest wall excursion
· Fingers: Mid-lateral incisions bilaterally

Signs indicating need: ↑ compartment pressure · ↓ Pulses · Tense oedema · ↑ Peak airway pressures (chest burns)

Timing: Do not delay — perform within hours of presentation if indicated

Fasciotomy: If muscle compartment involved (electrical burns, 4th degree) — deeper than escharotomy through fascia

⚡ Special Burns

ELECTRICAL BURNS
Entry + exit wounds. Internal injury far greater than surface area suggests.
Rhabdomyolysis → myoglobinuria → AKI
UO target: 1–2 ml/kg/hr
Cardiac monitoring 24h (arrhythmias)
ECG on arrival
CHEMICAL BURNS
Continuous damage until chemical removed.
Irrigate copiously with water — minimum 30 min
Do NOT neutralise (exothermic reaction worsens injury)
HF acid: calcium gluconate gel + systemic Ca²⁺

🏥 Burns Unit Referral Criteria (ABA)

· Partial thickness burns >10% TBSA
· Burns involving face, hands, feet, genitalia, perineum, major joints
· Full thickness burns any size
· Electrical or lightning burns
· Chemical burns
· Inhalation injury
· Burns in patients with pre-existing medical comorbidities
· Burns with concomitant trauma
· Suspected non-accidental injury (children)
· Circumferential burns of limbs or chest

🎯 Examiner Pearls

Q: Parkland gives 12.8L — patient's urine output is 2ml/kg/hr at hour 6. What do you do?
Reduce the infusion rate. Target UO is 0.5–1 ml/kg/hr. UO of 2ml/kg/hr = over-resuscitation → fluid creep → risk of abdominal compartment syndrome, pulmonary oedema. Titrate DOWN. Parkland is a guide, not a prescription.
Q: SpO₂ 99% in a house fire victim — reassured?
No. Pulse oximetry cannot distinguish carboxyhaemoglobin from oxyhaemoglobin — SpO₂ is falsely normal in CO poisoning. Perform co-oximetry on ABG to measure COHb directly. Give 100% O₂ empirically while awaiting results.
Q: Why use Hartmann's (Lactated Ringer's) and NOT 0.9% NaCl in burns?
Large volumes of 0.9% NaCl → hyperchloraemic metabolic acidosis. Hartmann's is more physiologically balanced (Na 130, Cl 109, lactate 28, K 4, Ca 2). Lactate is metabolised to bicarbonate — buffering the metabolic acidosis of burns shock. Normal saline worsens the acid-base picture.
Q: When do you give colloid in burns resuscitation?
After 24 hours. In the first 24 hours, the glycocalyx is disrupted and capillaries are hyperpermeable — colloid leaks into interstitium and worsens oedema. After 24 hours, capillary integrity is partially restored → colloid (albumin 5%) can be given to maintain oncotic pressure and reduce total fluid requirements.
