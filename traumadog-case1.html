📋 Clinical Vignette

A 29-year-old woman at 28 weeks gestation arrives after a pedestrian RTA. GCS 13. Pupils equal and reactive. Self-maintaining airway. CT head: bifrontal contusions with surrounding oedema — no midline shift, no haemorrhage requiring decompression. MRI spine: normal. Uterine artery Doppler bilateral PI 0.94. CTG: reactive pattern, no decelerations. She deteriorates to GCS 9 over the next 12 hours.

01 Left Lateral Tilt + ABC Primary Survey
Why tilt? The gravid uterus compresses the IVC in the supine position → reduced venous return → maternal hypotension → fetal compromise. Left lateral 15–30° or manual uterine displacement is mandatory from ≥20 weeks gestation.

Assess airway: self-maintained? FRC is reduced 20% in late pregnancy — desaturates faster than a non-pregnant patient. Pre-oxygenate aggressively before any instrumentation.

03 Ventilation — Target PaCO₂ 30–32 mmHg
This is the most common exam trap. Normal PaCO₂ in the third trimester is 30–32 mmHg, not 40 mmHg. Progesterone drives a compensatory respiratory alkalosis in pregnancy.

Ventilating to PaCO₂ 40 mmHg = hypercarbia relative to her physiological baseline → worsening cerebral oedema.

Avoid aggressive hyperventilation below 30 mmHg → cerebral vasoconstriction → reduced uteroplacental perfusion.

Mode: PRVC. Head elevation 30°. Sedation: propofol + fentanyl infusion.
tap to expand ▾
04 Anti-oedema Therapy — HTS preferred over Mannitol
Mannitol: crosses the placenta → fetal hyperosmolality and hypovolaemia. Not absolutely contraindicated (maternal herniation kills both patients) but use lowest effective bolus 0.5 g/kg.

Hypertonic saline (3% NaCl): preferred in pregnancy — does not cross the placenta to the same degree, haemodynamically well tolerated, equally effective for ICP control.

05 Seizure Prophylaxis + TXA + Fetal Monitoring
Levetiracetam 500 mg IV BD — drug of choice. Category C; renally excreted; predictable PK.

Valproate is Category D — CONTRAINDICATED. Teratogenic: neural tube defects, cognitive impairment, neonatal withdrawal.

TXA: CRASH-3 supports use within 3 hours in mild-to-moderate TBI. WOMAN trial data reassuring in maternal haemorrhage. TXA 2 g IV over 30 min used here.

CTG 4-hourly. Hydroxyprogesterone 500 mg IM to reduce preterm labour risk. MDT (obstetrics, neurosurgery, neonatology) within first hour.

Outcome: Weaned ventilation Day 8. GCS 15 by Day 12. Delivered electively 34 weeks by CS under spinal.

🎤 Viva Questions

Why is PaCO₂ 40 mmHg harmful in a ventilated pregnant TBI patient? ▶
Progesterone drives a compensatory respiratory alkalosis in pregnancy. Normal third-trimester PaCO₂ is 30–32 mmHg. Ventilating to 40 mmHg means you are hypercarbing her relative to her physiological baseline — this causes cerebral vasodilation and worsening oedema.
Why choose hypertonic saline over mannitol in this case? ▶
Mannitol crosses the placenta and can produce fetal hyperosmolality and hypovolaemia. Hypertonic saline has lower placental transfer, is haemodynamically well tolerated, and achieves equivalent ICP control. Mannitol is not absolutely contraindicated — herniation kills both patients — but HTS is the preferred first-line agent.
What is the significance of FRC reduction in pregnancy for RSI? ▶
FRC is reduced by ~20% at term because the gravid uterus elevates the diaphragm. The oxygen reserve during apnoea is smaller — the pregnant patient desaturates significantly faster during the apnoeic window of RSI. Thorough pre-oxygenation (5 min 100% FiO₂) and minimising apnoeic time are essential.
Why was valproate not used despite being a familiar anticonvulsant? ▶
Sodium valproate is FDA Category D — associated with neural tube defects, cognitive impairment in exposed children, and neonatal withdrawal. Levetiracetam (Category C, renally excreted, predictable pharmacokinetics) is the anticonvulsant of choice in TBI during pregnancy when a safer alternative exists.
