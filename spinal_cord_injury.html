⚡ Neurogenic Shock vs Spinal Shock — KNOW THIS COLD

NEUROGENIC SHOCK
What: Haemodynamic entity — distributive shock
Level: Injury above T6
Mechanism: Loss of descending sympathetic pathways → ↓ vasomotor tone → vasodilation + pooling → hypotension + bradycardia (unopposed vagal tone)
Classic: Hypotension WITHOUT tachycardia, warm peripheries
Duration: Up to 5 weeks
Treatment: Vasopressors (noradrenaline/dopamine) + fluids cautiously + atropine for bradycardia
SPINAL SHOCK
What: Neurological syndrome — NOT haemodynamic
Level: Any level
Mechanism: Transient loss of spinal reflex activity below injury level
Features: Flaccid paralysis, areflexia, loss of sensation, loss of bladder/bowel
Resolution: Return of bulbocavernosus reflex at 48–72h = end of spinal shock
Treatment: Supportive — no specific haemodynamic treatment needed
⚠ KEY EXAM TRAP: "Spinal shock" does NOT mean haemodynamic shock. It is purely a neurological phenomenon. Neurogenic shock is the haemodynamic emergency. Don't confuse them.

Neurogenic shock below T6? → Look for another cause — haemorrhage most likely. TSCI below T6 rarely causes neurogenic shock.

💉 Haemodynamic Management — TSCI Protocol

STEP 1 — EXCLUDE HAEMORRHAGE FIRST
Hypotension in TSCI = haemorrhage until proven otherwise. FAST scan. Check for other injuries. Do NOT assume neurogenic shock without excluding bleeding.
↓
STEP 2 — FLUID RESUSCITATION
Judicious isotonic crystalloid (0.9% NaCl or Hartmann's). Avoid hypotonic fluids (D5W, 0.45% NaCl) — cause spinal cord oedema. Avoid over-resuscitation — cervical/high thoracic injuries prone to pulmonary oedema (↓ sympathetic cardiac input).
↓
STEP 3 — VASOPRESSORS
Target MAP 85–90 mmHg for 7 days (AANS guidelines — optimises spinal cord perfusion).
First line: Noradrenaline (vasoconstrictive + inotropic)
Above T6: consider Dopamine (vasoconstrictive + chronotropic — helps bradycardia)
Atropine or external pacing for haemodynamically significant bradycardia
MAP target: 85–90 mmHg for 7 days — spinal cord perfusion pressure = MAP − CSFP. Autoregulation lost after TSCI → cord becomes pressure-passive. Maintaining MAP directly maintains cord perfusion.

🫁 Airway Considerations

C3–C5: Phrenic nerve — diaphragm paralysis → immediate ventilatory failure. Intubate.
C5–T1: Intercostal + accessory muscle loss → reduced respiratory reserve. Monitor closely.
Above C5: High probability of requiring mechanical ventilation.

Intubation technique: MILS (manual in-line stabilisation) — NOT traction. CMAC/video laryngoscopy preferred. Awake fibreoptic if time allows and patient cooperative.

Suxamethonium: Safe within first 72 hours. AVOID between Day 3 and 9 months — upregulation of extrajunctional ACh receptors → life-threatening hyperkalaemia.

🧠 Incomplete SCI Syndromes

CENTRAL CORD
Most common incomplete SCI. Hyperextension injury. Upper limbs > lower limbs weakness. Bladder dysfunction. Elderly + cervical spondylosis.
Prognosis: Best of incomplete syndromes.
ANTERIOR CORD
Flexion injury / vascular (anterior spinal artery). Loss of motor + pain/temp below. Preserved proprioception + vibration (posterior columns intact).
Prognosis: Worst of incomplete syndromes.
BROWN-SÉQUARD
Hemisection of cord (penetrating trauma). Ipsilateral: motor loss + proprioception/vibration loss. Contralateral: pain/temp loss.
Prognosis: Good — most recovery.
POSTERIOR CORD
Rare. Loss of proprioception + vibration + fine touch below. Motor and pain/temp preserved.
Prognosis: Variable.

📊 SLIC Score — Subaxial Cervical Injury

Morphology: No injury (0) · Compression (1) · Burst (2) · Distraction (3) · Rotation/translation (4)
DLC (Disc-Ligament Complex): Intact (0) · Indeterminate (1) · Disrupted (2)
Neurological status: Intact (0) · Root injury (1) · Complete cord (2) · Incomplete cord (3) · Ongoing compression +1

Score ≤3: Non-operative (immobilisation)
Score = 4: Indeterminate — surgeon judgement
Score ≥5: Operative — surgical stabilisation

🔍 Cervical Spine Clearance Rules

NEXUS CRITERIA — Low Risk = No Imaging Needed
ALL 5 must be present to clear clinically (no CT/X-ray needed):

1. No midline cervical tenderness
2. No focal neurological deficit
3. Normal level of alertness (GCS 15)
4. No intoxication (alcohol or drugs)
5. No painful distracting injury

If ALL 5 present → low risk → no imaging required → can remove collar.
If ANY criterion absent → image the spine.

Sensitivity: 99.6% · Specificity: 12.9% — high sensitivity, low specificity (many false positives)
CANADIAN C-SPINE RULE (CCR)
Step 1 — Any HIGH RISK factor? → Image immediately:
· Age ≥65
· Dangerous mechanism (fall >1m/5 stairs, axial load to head, high-speed MVC, rollover, ejection, motorised recreational vehicle, bicycle collision)
· Paraesthesia in extremities

Step 2 — Any LOW RISK factor allowing safe ROM assessment?
· Simple rear-end MVC
· Sitting position in ED
· Ambulatory at any time
· Delayed onset neck pain
· Absence of midline C-spine tenderness

Step 3 — Can patient actively rotate neck 45° left AND right?
Yes → No imaging needed
No → Image required

Sensitivity: 99.4% · Specificity: 45.1% — more specific than NEXUS
Not applicable: GCS <15, unstable vitals, age <16, known vertebral disease, previous C-spine surgery

⚡ SCIWORA

SCIWORA — Spinal Cord Injury Without Radiological Abnormality
Definition: Objective signs of myelopathy (neurological deficit) with no evidence of fracture or malalignment on plain X-rays AND CT scan.

Mechanism: Cord injury from transient compression, vascular insult, or disc herniation without bony injury. Cord elastic deformation exceeds physiological limits.

Who gets it:
· Children — hypermobile ligamentous spine, small vertebral bodies → cord can be compressed without bony injury (up to 50% of paediatric SCI)
· Adults with cervical spondylosis — pre-existing canal stenosis
· Hyperextension injuries in elderly

Diagnosis: MRI is mandatory — may show cord contusion, haematoma, oedema, disc herniation not visible on CT. Normal MRI does not exclude SCIWORA.

Management: Immobilise · MRI urgently · Neurosurgical review · Same haemodynamic targets as other TSCI (MAP 85–90 mmHg)

Prognosis: Variable — complete neurological deficit = poor. Partial deficit = better recovery possible.

👶 Paediatric Spinal Cord Injury — Key Differences

Anatomy differences:
· Large head relative to body → fulcrum of injury at C2–C3 (adults C5–C6)
· Hypermobile ligamentous spine → greater range of motion → SCIWORA more common
· Horizontal facet joints → more translational injuries
· Vertebral bodies incompletely ossified → pseudosubluxation can mimic injury

Pseudosubluxation: Normal physiological anterior displacement of C2 on C3 (up to 3mm) and C3 on C4 in children <8 years — do NOT mistake for injury. Use Swischuk's line to differentiate.

SCIWORA prevalence: Up to 50% of paediatric SCI — always MRI if neurological signs with normal CT.

NEXUS/CCR in children: NEXUS validated from age 2+. CCR NOT validated under 16 — use clinical judgement + NEXUS in children.

Immobilisation: Standard adult backboard causes neck flexion in children (large occiput) → use occipital recess board or pad under torso to maintain neutral alignment.

Suxamethonium: Same 72h rule applies — safe initially, avoid after Day 3 to 9 months post-injury.

Haemodynamic targets: MAP targets lower than adults — age-appropriate. Avoid aggressive fluid resuscitation — risk of pulmonary oedema greater.

⚠️ Autonomic Dysreflexia — Late Complication

AUTONOMIC DYSREFLEXIA (T6 and above)
Trigger: Noxious stimulus below level of injury — most common: bladder distension or faecal impaction

Mechanism: Massive sympathetic discharge below injury level → vasoconstriction + hypertension. Compensatory parasympathetic response above level → bradycardia + vasodilation (flushing, sweating above injury).

Clinical: Severe hypertensive crisis · Bradycardia · Pounding headache · Flushing/sweating above level · Pallor below level · Nasal congestion · Anxiety

Complications: MI · Intracranial haemorrhage · Stroke · Seizures · Pulmonary oedema · Retinal haemorrhage

Treatment:
1. Sit patient up (↓ BP orthostatic)
2. Find and remove trigger — catheterise if in retention, relieve faecal impaction
3. Antihypertensives: GTN spray/patch · Labetalol · Hydralazine · Nifedipine
4. Atropine/glycopyrrolate for bradycardia
5. Prophylaxis: oral nifedipine or prazosin

🚫 Steroids in TSCI

Methylprednisolone (NASCIS trials): Once recommended — now NOT recommended in acute TSCI.

Evidence of increased sepsis, pneumonia, GI bleeding, and ICU complications without clear neurological benefit.

Current AANS/ATLS guidelines: Steroids are not standard of care in acute TSCI.

🎯 Examiner Pearls

Q: Neurogenic shock vs haemorrhagic shock — how do you differentiate?
Neurogenic shock: hypotension + bradycardia + warm peripheries + no tachycardia. Haemorrhagic shock: hypotension + tachycardia + cold clammy peripheries. But ALWAYS exclude haemorrhage first — it is far more common and both can coexist.
Q: Why avoid suxamethonium after 72 hours in TSCI?
Denervation → upregulation of extrajunctional nicotinic ACh receptors across the entire muscle membrane. Suxamethonium depolarises ALL these receptors simultaneously → massive K⁺ efflux → life-threatening hyperkalaemia → cardiac arrest. Avoid from Day 3 to 9 months post-injury. Use rocuronium + sugammadex instead.
Q: Why is MAP target 85–90 mmHg (not 65 mmHg) in TSCI?
Spinal cord perfusion pressure = MAP − CSFP. After TSCI, spinal cord autoregulation is lost — cord becomes pressure-passive. A higher MAP target directly maintains cord perfusion pressure, preventing secondary ischaemic injury. Evidence (AANS Class 3) supports 7 days of MAP ≥85 mmHg to optimise neurological recovery.
Q: What is sacral sparing and why does it matter?
Preservation of any sacral function (perianal sensation, voluntary anal sphincter contraction, great toe flexion) = incomplete injury = ASIA B or better. Critical distinction: complete vs incomplete changes prognosis dramatically. Always check sacral function before classifying a cord injury as complete.
