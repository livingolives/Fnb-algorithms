Percutaneous Dilatational Tracheostomy

PDT · Anatomy · Seldinger Technique · Complications

🧬 Surgical Anatomy & Insertion Level

Anterior Neck — Sagittal View
Insertion between 1st–2nd or 2nd–3rd tracheal rings, in the midline.
Too high (cricoid/1st ring) → subglottic stenosis. Too low → tracheo-innominate artery erosion. The thyroid isthmus may overlie the site and be a source of bleeding.

🚫 Contraindications

Absolute
Uncontrolled / uncorrectable coagulopathy
Infection at insertion site
Unable to identify landmarks
Children (small airway) — relative in some texts
Lack of trained operator / equipment
Relative
Obesity / short neck / difficult anatomy
Correctable coagulopathy
High vent requirement (PEEP >10, FiO₂ >0.6)
Raised ICP — procedure spikes ICP (coughing, ↑CO₂)
C-spine injury (can't extend neck)
Previous neck surgery/radiation, goitre, high innominate artery
In the TBI patient specifically: raised ICP is the key concern. Coughing and hypercapnia during the procedure spike ICP. Optimise ICP first, deepen sedation, paralyse, and control ventilation to avoid PaCO₂ surges.

🪡 Seldinger Technique — Step by Step

Modified Seldinger (Ciaglia Single Dilator)
Throughout: continuous bronchoscopy via the withdrawn ETT confirms midline puncture between correct rings and that wire/dilator/tube enter the true lumen — not the posterior wall or a false passage.
1
Preparation
Position — neck extended (shoulder roll) unless C-spine injury. Pre-oxygenate 100%. Deepen sedation + paralyse. Withdraw ETT under bronchoscopic vision so cuff sits just below cords. Identify landmarks: cricoid → 1st–3rd rings, midline.
2
Needle Puncture
Introducer needle midline between 1st–2nd or 2nd–3rd rings, aspirating for air to confirm tracheal entry. Bronchoscope confirms midline and level, avoids posterior wall.
3
Guidewire (Seldinger)
J-tipped guidewire passed through needle into trachea, directed caudally. Needle removed, wire remains. Everything now rail-roads over this wire.
4
Dilatation
Guiding catheter over wire, then progressive dilatation. Ciaglia single tapered dilator (most common) or Griggs forceps technique. Tract dilated to size of tracheostomy tube.
5
Tube Insertion & Confirmation
Tracheostomy tube loaded on dilator, rail-roaded over wire into trachea. Remove wire + dilator. Inflate cuff. Confirm: EtCO₂ trace, bilateral air entry, bronchoscopic view of tube in lumen. Only then remove the ETT fully.

🔬 Why Bronchoscopy is Non-Negotiable

Continuous fibreoptic bronchoscopy through the withdrawn ETT is what makes PDT safer than a blind technique. It prevents:

• Posterior tracheal wall perforation → tracheo-oesophageal fistula
• False passage — confirms wire/dilator/tube in true lumen
• Wrong level — confirms puncture between correct rings, midline
• Paratracheal placement → vascular injury

Ultrasound is increasingly used pre-procedure to identify midline, vessels (anterior jugular veins, high innominate artery), and thyroid isthmus.

⚠️ Complications

Early / Intraoperative
▸ Haemorrhage (thyroid isthmus, ant. jugular veins)
▸ False passage / posterior wall injury
▸ Loss of airway / accidental extubation
▸ Hypoxia
▸ Pneumothorax / pneumomediastinum
▸ Subcutaneous emphysema
▸ Cuff puncture by needle
Late
▸ Tracheal stenosis
▸ Tracheomalacia
▸ Tube occlusion (mucus plug)
▸ Tracheo-oesophageal fistula
▸ Tracheo-innominate fistula
▸ Stomal infection / granuloma
⚡ Tracheo-Innominate Artery Fistula — Know This Cold
Occurs if tube placed too low or tip erodes the innominate (brachiocephalic) artery. Presents 1–6 weeks post-procedure.
Sentinel bleed (small herald bleed) precedes massive exsanguinating haemorrhage — never ignore a small tracheostomy bleed.
Management: hyperinflate cuff to tamponade → Utley manoeuvre (finger in stoma, press artery anteriorly against sternum) → immediate surgery. Mortality very high.

🚨 Desaturation During PDT — DOPES

Capnograph loss + desaturation during a shared-airway procedure. Stop the procedure, take airway control, hand-ventilate 100% O₂, use the bronchoscope to see where tube and trachea actually are. Work through DOPES:
D
Displacement — ETT withdrawn too far / accidental extubation. Most likely — check first.
O
Obstruction — tube blocked, cuff punctured by needle → massive leak, cuff herniation.
P
Pneumothorax — tension, from posterior wall injury or high insertion.
E
Equipment — circuit disconnection, capnograph line kinked, ventilator fault.
S
Stacking — breath stacking / auto-PEEP (less likely in this context).
In PDT specifically: displacement and cuff puncture are the two most likely causes — the airway is being actively manipulated and a needle is entering near the cuff. If you cannot ventilate via the existing tube, fully re-intubate past the puncture or convert to surgical airway.

🎯 Examiner Pearls

Q: PDT vs surgical tracheostomy — advantages of percutaneous?
Bedside (no transfer to theatre), faster, smaller incision, lower stomal infection and bleeding rates, better cosmesis, cost-effective. Surgical preferred for difficult anatomy, emergency, coagulopathy, or paediatric.
Q: Which tracheal level and why not higher or lower?
Between 1st–2nd or 2nd–3rd rings. Higher (cricoid/1st ring) risks subglottic stenosis. Lower risks tracheo-innominate artery erosion and difficult tube angulation.
Q: A patient 3 weeks post-tracheostomy has a small bleed from the stoma. Significance?
Sentinel bleed of tracheo-innominate fistula until proven otherwise. Even a small herald bleed mandates urgent investigation — it precedes catastrophic haemorrhage. Hyperinflate cuff, Utley manoeuvre, immediate surgical review.
Q: Why is PDT relatively contraindicated in raised ICP?
Coughing and hypercapnia during the procedure raise intrathoracic pressure and PaCO₂ → cerebral vasodilation → ICP spikes. In a TBI patient, optimise ICP first, ensure deep sedation and paralysis, and control ventilation tightly to prevent CO₂ surges.
