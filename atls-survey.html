ATLS Primary & Secondary Survey

ATLS 10th Ed · Separate Exam Station

📋 Primary Survey — ABCDE

A
Airway with C-Spine Protection
Assess patency, look for obstruction, listen for stridor. C-spine immobilised throughout — manual in-line stabilisation before collar if uncertain.
B
Breathing with Ventilation
Expose chest fully. Look for tension pneumothorax: tracheal shift, distended neck veins, absent breath sounds, hyperresonant percussion. Assess rate, symmetry, effort.
C
Circulation with Haemorrhage Control
Identify and control exsanguinating external wounds (direct pressure, tourniquet). Establish IV access, send bloods, initiate resuscitation/blood products. Assess pulse, cap refill, skin colour.
D
Disability
GCS (or AVPU rapid screen). Pupils — size, reactivity, compare both sides. Gross motor/sensory screen.
E
Exposure / Environment
Fully undress patient. Log roll with C-spine control + apply pelvic binder if indicated. Prevent hypothermia — warm blankets/fluids immediately after exposure.

🔧 Adjuncts to Primary Survey

A distinct, formally named ATLS section — separate from the ABCDE letters themselves. Examiners ask this standalone: "name the adjuncts to primary survey."
▸ Monitoring — continuous ECG, pulse oximetry, BP, EtCO₂ if intubated
▸ Urinary catheter — assess output/resuscitation adequacy. Contraindicated if blood at meatus, high-riding/boggy prostate, scrotal haematoma, or pelvic fracture — do retrograde urethrogram first
▸ Gastric catheter — decompresses stomach, reduces aspiration risk. Use orogastric, not nasogastric, if suspected base of skull fracture (cribriform plate injury risk)
▸ Trauma series X-rays — chest, pelvis (± lateral C-spine; many centres now proceed straight to CT instead of plain film series)
▸ FAST/eFAST — adjunct to circulation assessment, performed during primary survey
▸ ABG + lactate — assess adequacy of resuscitation and ventilation
▸ Consider need for transfer if local capability is exceeded — arrange early, don't wait until deterioration

🔧 Adjuncts to Secondary Survey

▸ Detailed imaging as indicated by findings — CT head/C-spine/chest/abdomen/pelvis, specific limb films
▸ Specialist consultation as injuries are identified — neurosurgery, orthopaedics, OMFS etc.
▸ Ongoing monitoring and reassessment — vital signs, urine output, response to interventions
⚠ Exam Structure Reminder
"Adjuncts" are a formally separate ATLS category from the primary/secondary survey steps themselves — if asked to "describe the primary survey," adjuncts are typically a follow-up question, not automatically included unless specifically asked to "include adjuncts."
GCS ≤8 → Intubate. Threshold for securing the airway due to risk of aspiration and loss of airway reflexes. Say "≤8" not just "=8" — applies to GCS 8, 7, 6... down to 3.

⏱ Secondary Survey — Timing

Secondary survey begins only AFTER the primary survey is complete and resuscitation is underway — not before, and not run as a simultaneous parallel process. Continuous reassessment of primary survey findings happens throughout the secondary survey, but the secondary survey itself does not start until ABCDE has been addressed and the patient is stabilising.

🔍 Secondary Survey — Head to Toe Checklist

▸ Reassess all primary survey findings continuously
▸ Remove constricting rings/jewellery at fingers before swelling progresses
▸ Scalp — laceration, boggy swelling. Ear/nose — CSF leak, bleeding (basal skull #). Oral — missing teeth (aspiration risk)
▸ Meatus — blood at urethral meatus suggests urethral injury; do NOT catheterise until excluded
▸ During log roll: examine spine for stepoff/tenderness, anal region for bleeding/tone (spinal injury, penetrating rectal injury)
▸ Deglove/retroperitoneal signs: Cullen's sign (periumbilical ecchymosis), Grey Turner's sign (flank ecchymosis), Fox's sign (scrotal/inguinal ecchymosis) — all suggest retroperitoneal haemorrhage
▸ Full limb exam — deformity, pulses, compartments, neurovascular status distal to any fracture

🔄 Log Roll Technique

Minimum Team
4–5
Command
"On 3"
1 at head — controls airway/C-spine, gives the count, coordinates the roll. 2 at each side (or 1 side + 1 at pelvis/legs) — roll the torso as one unit. 1 at foot end if pelvic binder being applied simultaneously. On count of 3, entire body turns as a single unit — no independent movement of spine segments.

🎯 Examiner Pearls

Q: What's the difference between the "immediate six" and "hidden six" chest injuries?
Immediate six are identified and treated on primary survey — they kill within minutes if missed (airway obstruction, tension/open pneumothorax, massive haemothorax, flail chest, tamponade). Hidden six are found on secondary survey — serious but not immediately lethal in the same timeframe (simple pneumothorax, haemothorax, pulmonary contusion, tracheobronchial injury, blunt cardiac injury, aortic disruption, plus diaphragmatic and oesophageal injury).
Q: Blood at the urethral meatus — what do you NOT do?
Do not attempt urethral catheterisation until urethral injury is excluded (retrograde urethrogram). Catheterising a transected urethra can convert a partial injury into a complete one.
Q: Name the three cutaneous signs of retroperitoneal haemorrhage.
Cullen's sign (periumbilical ecchymosis), Grey Turner's sign (flank ecchymosis), Fox's sign (scrotal/inguinal ligament ecchymosis). All are late signs — may take hours to days to appear, do not rule out retroperitoneal bleed if absent early.
