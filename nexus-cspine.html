NEXUS & Canadian C-Spine Rule

Clinical Clearance of the Cervical Spine

📋 NEXUS Criteria — All 5 Must Be Met to Clear

1
No midline cervical tenderness
2
No focal neurological deficit
3
Normal level of alertness
4
No intoxication
5
No distracting injury — a separate painful injury (long bone #, major laceration, burn) that would distract from reporting neck pain. NOT the crash mechanism itself.
⚠ Age is NOT a NEXUS criterion
The single most common exam confusion — age belongs to Canadian C-Spine Rule, not NEXUS.
If ALL FIVE criteria are met (i.e. none of the concerning features present) → clinically clear, no imaging needed. Fail even ONE → imaging required.

🍁 Canadian C-Spine Rule — Three-Step Decision Tree

Step 1 — High-Risk Factors (ANY one → image, stop here)
▸ Age ≥65
▸ Dangerous mechanism (fall >3ft/5 stairs, axial load, high-speed MVC, rollover, ejection, motorised recreational vehicle, bicycle collision)
▸ Paraesthesias in extremities
↓ none present
Step 2 — Low-Risk Factors (safety gate — allows ROM testing)
▸ Simple rear-end MVC
▸ Sitting position in ED
▸ Ambulatory at any time since injury
▸ Delayed onset of neck pain
▸ Absence of midline C-spine tenderness
If ZERO present → too many concerning features, unsafe to test movement → image directly, skip Step 3.
↓ ≥1 present
Step 3 — Active Range of Motion Test
▸ Can patient actively rotate neck 45° LEFT and RIGHT?
✓ Full, pain-free rotation → clear clinically, no imaging
✗ Restricted or painful → image
⚠ Common Misunderstanding
Low-risk factors are not a clearance criterion themselves — they're a safety gate deciding whether it's safe to attempt the ROM test. "Sitting in ED" alone does NOT mean "no imaging" — it means "safe enough to now test neck rotation, and THAT result determines the imaging decision."

🚨 Cannot Clinically Assess — Intubated/GCS Low

Neither rule can be applied to a patient who cannot be examined — can't assess alertness, can't test ROM, can't reliably assess tenderness or neuro deficit.

CT cervical spine is the modality of choice — far higher sensitivity than plain X-ray series in the obtunded/intubated trauma patient, now standard of care over 3-view X-ray.

Maintain manual in-line stabilisation / hard collar until CT reported clear. Clinical clearance can be reconsidered later once the patient is extubated/alert enough to be examined if CT was unavailable, but CT is the primary expected answer.

🎯 Examiner Pearls

Q: Is age part of NEXUS criteria?
No. This is the most common exam confusion. NEXUS has five criteria — midline tenderness, focal neuro deficit, alertness, intoxication, distracting injury — none involve age. Age ≥65 is a Canadian C-Spine Rule high-risk factor.
Q: A patient has one low-risk factor (ambulatory since injury) but you haven't tested ROM yet — can you clear them?
Not yet. The low-risk factor only makes it safe to proceed to Step 3 (active ROM testing). Clearance depends on the outcome of that test — full pain-free rotation clears them, restricted/painful rotation means imaging.
Q: Why is Canadian C-Spine Rule considered more sensitive but less specific than NEXUS?
It catches more true injuries (higher sensitivity) partly because age ≥65 alone triggers mandatory imaging regardless of clinical findings — but this also means more patients without actual injury get imaged unnecessarily (lower specificity) compared to NEXUS's pure symptom-based checklist.
