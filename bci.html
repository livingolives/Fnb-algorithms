Blunt Cardiac Injury

Spectrum · Screening · ECG Findings · Monitoring
Case: Steering wheel injury, head-on MVC, sternal bruising, haemodynamically stable, mild tachycardia. Sternal fracture and rib fractures are associated mechanism markers — they raise suspicion for BCI but are NOT part of the BCI spectrum itself.

📊 Spectrum of Injury — Mild to Severe

1
Myocardial Contusion
Bruising of myocardium — most common form, often asymptomatic/subclinical.
2
Myocardial Stunning / Dysfunction
Transient contractile dysfunction, arrhythmias.
3
Coronary Artery Injury
Dissection/thrombosis from blunt force — can cause true myocardial infarction.
4
Valve / Septal Rupture
Structural damage — acute regurgitation, new murmur.
5
Cardiac Rupture / Tamponade
Free wall rupture — often immediately fatal at scene.
✦
Commotio Cordis — Mechanistically Distinct
Precordial impact during the vulnerable repolarisation window (upslope of T wave) triggers VF in a structurally NORMAL heart. Timing-dependent, not force/injury-dependent — not part of the severity spectrum, a separate entity.

🔍 Screening Pathway

Step 1 — 12-Lead ECG
Initial screening test of choice (EAST guidelines) — NOT troponin first. Look for: new arrhythmia, ST changes, heart block, unexplained sinus tachycardia.
↓
Step 2 — Troponin
Adjunct to ECG, not a replacement. Assay-specific cutoffs — quote as "above local lab's upper reference limit" rather than a fixed number, since conventional vs high-sensitivity assays differ significantly.
↓
Combined Result
Normal ECG + Normal Troponin = very high negative predictive value (~95-100%) for clinically significant BCI. This combination allows safe exclusion without further workup.

📈 ECG Findings — Labelled Traces

Normal Sinus Rhythm — Reference
Normal P-QRS-T. Regular rate, uniform QRS amplitude beat to beat.
RBBB — Right Bundle Branch Block
Most common significant conduction finding in BCI — RV is the most anterior chamber, directly behind the sternum, most exposed to blunt force. Widened QRS with RSR' pattern (M-shaped) in V1.
Atrial Fibrillation
Irregularly irregular R-R intervals, absent discrete P waves, fibrillatory baseline. Second classic pathological BCI arrhythmia after RBBB.
Electrical Alternans (Tamponade — Not BCI Itself)
Not a BCI finding — this is a pericardial effusion/tamponade sign. QRS amplitude alternates beat-to-beat because the heart physically swings within a large effusion, changing its electrical axis relative to the recording electrodes. Include here only to distinguish from BCI's own findings.

🎯 Monitoring Decision

Normal ECG + Normal Trop
Safe to discharge/de-escalate without further cardiac monitoring.
Either Abnormal
Admit for continuous ECG monitoring 24–48 hours. Significant arrhythmias almost always manifest in this window.
After 24–48 hours without arrhythmia → safe to de-escalate monitoring. Most common arrhythmia overall: sinus tachycardia (often just physiological response to injury/pain, not itself diagnostic of BCI). Most common pathological arrhythmias: RBBB and atrial fibrillation.
⚠ Examiner Traps
Troponin is the first screening test → 12-lead ECG is first-line; troponin is an adjunct
Sternal fracture = BCI → Sternal/rib fractures are mechanism markers raising suspicion — not part of the BCI spectrum itself
Electrical alternans is a BCI finding → It's a pericardial effusion/tamponade sign — separate entity
Commotio cordis is severe blunt injury → It's a timing-dependent arrhythmia in a structurally NORMAL heart — mechanistically distinct from the injury severity spectrum

🎯 Examiner Pearls

Q: Why is RBBB the classic conduction finding in BCI?
The right ventricle is the most anterior cardiac chamber, sitting directly behind the sternum — making it the most exposed to blunt anterior chest force (e.g. steering wheel injury). This anatomical exposure explains why right-sided conduction pathway injury (RBBB) is disproportionately common compared to left-sided blocks.
Q: What does a normal ECG and normal troponin at presentation tell you?
Very high negative predictive value (~95-100%) for clinically significant BCI — this combination allows safe exclusion of BCI without echo or extended monitoring, per EAST guidelines.
Q: How is commotio cordis different from the rest of the BCI spectrum?
It's not force/injury-dependent like contusion-to-rupture — it's timing-dependent. A relatively minor precordial impact during the vulnerable window of repolarisation (upslope of the T wave, ~15-30ms) triggers VF in an otherwise structurally normal heart. This is why young athletes hit by a ball/puck can arrest despite no structural cardiac damage.
