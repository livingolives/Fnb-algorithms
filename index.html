𓁹 ANUBIS & THE ANAESTHETIST 𓁹
In ancient Egyptian mythology, Anubis — the jackal-headed god — was the psychopomp: the divine guide of souls across the threshold between the living world and the realm of the dead. He did not choose who died. He simply ensured that those who crossed over did so with dignity, guided safely through the unknown.
The anaesthetist walks a strikingly similar path. Every time a patient crosses the threshold of consciousness — surrendering awareness, sensation, and control — it is the anaesthetist who stands at the border. We guide them in. We guide them out. The journey through anaesthesia is, in many ways, a controlled passage through the unknown: a temporary dissolution of self, held safely in our hands.
Unlike Anubis, we do not guide people to the afterlife. And it is a very bad day when we do. Instead, our purpose is a smooth, peaceful perioperative course — into the deep, and back again, with the patient intact in body and in soul.
Trauma anaesthesia adds another dimension entirely. These patients arrive broken — sometimes shattered — in body and in spirit. The trauma anaesthetist must hold the physiological chaos together long enough for healing to begin. Broken bones come with broken souls. We treat both.
"He who guides the living through the unknown
bears the same duty as he who guides the dead —
to carry the soul safely across."
— ADAPTED FROM EGYPTIAN FUNERARY TEXTS
