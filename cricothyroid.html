Cricothyroid Membrane

Anatomy · CICO · Scalpel-Bougie-Tube Technique

🧬 Anatomical Boundaries

Anterior Neck — Laryngeal Cartilages
Boundaries: superiorly attached to thyroid cartilage, inferiorly to cricoid cartilage, free laterally. Size ~9mm (height) × 30mm (width) — large enough for scalpel-bougie-tube technique, not a tiny 4mm gap.

📐 Boundaries

Superior
Thyroid cartilage (lower border)
Inferior
Cricoid cartilage (upper border)
Lateral
Free — no bony/cartilaginous boundary, cricothyroid muscle lies over it laterally
Pierced by
Internal branch of superior laryngeal nerve (sensory to supraglottic larynx)
Naming note: always "cricothyroid" membrane — not "thyrocricoid." Anatomical nomenclature convention (Terminologia Anatomica), not derived from superior-to-inferior logic. Same pattern in cricothyroid muscle, cricothyroid ligament, cricothyroidotomy.

⚠️ Structures at Risk

Cricothyroid artery
Runs transversely across the upper third of the membrane. This is why the incision is made in the lower half.
SLN internal branch
Pierces the membrane — sensory to supraglottic larynx. Risk with lateral straying.
Vocal cords
If incision/tube placement strays too high, into thyroid cartilage/laryngeal inlet.
Thyroid isthmus
If too low, below cricoid — bleeding risk. Mirror of the PDT "too low" complication.
Oesophagus
If scalpel/bougie passed too deep — posterior wall perforation, same principle as PDT.
Recurrent laryngeal nerve
More lateral/posterior — lower risk with correct midline technique but relevant if straying significantly.

🔪 DAS Scalpel-Bougie-Tube Technique

True CICO emergency: skip LA infiltration entirely. Seconds matter. LA/aspiration steps are only for a controlled or semi-elective front-of-neck access, not genuine can't-intubate-can't-oxygenate.
1
Identify Landmarks
Extend neck if no C-spine concern. Palpate midline. Laryngeal handshake technique if landmarks unclear (common in obesity/oedema).
2
Horizontal Stab Incision
Through skin and cricothyroid membrane in one motion, in the lower half of the membrane to avoid the cricothyroid artery above.
3
Rotate & Open Tract
Rotate scalpel 90° (blade caudally) to open the incision, or maintain with finger palpation/retractor to keep the tract patent.
4
Bougie
Insert bougie alongside the blade, angled caudally into the trachea — feel for tracheal clicks/hold-up confirming correct placement.
5
Railroad Tube
Size 6.0 cuffed tube railroaded over the bougie into the trachea.
6
Confirm & Secure
Inflate cuff, confirm with EtCO₂ and bilateral chest rise/air entry. Secure tube.
7
Elective Conversion
Plan conversion to formal surgical tracheostomy with ENT once oxygenation is secured — this is a rescue airway, not a definitive one.

💡 Why Scalpel-Bougie-Tube, Not Needle Cricothyroidotomy

The membrane's actual size (~9×30mm — far larger than commonly underestimated 4mm) is large enough for a direct scalpel incision and tube placement. DAS 2015 moved away from needle cricothyroidotomy toward scalpel-bougie-tube as first-line in CICO because:

• Needle technique uses narrow-gauge cannula — prone to kinking, dislodgement
• Requires jet ventilation — high barotrauma/surgical emphysema risk
• Higher failure and complication rate in real-world CICO series
• Scalpel technique has a higher first-pass success rate and is faster once trained
⚠ Examiner Traps
Membrane is ~4mm → ~9mm × 30mm — large enough for direct scalpel technique
"Thyrocricoid membrane" → Always "cricothyroid membrane" — fixed anatomical convention
LA infiltration before incision in true CICO → Skip LA entirely in genuine emergency — seconds matter
Incision in upper half of membrane → Lower half — avoids cricothyroid artery running across upper third

🎯 Examiner Pearls

Q: Why is the incision made in the lower half of the membrane specifically?
The cricothyroid artery runs transversely across the upper third. A lower-half horizontal stab incision avoids significant arterial haemorrhage that would obscure the surgical field during a time-critical airway emergency.
Q: Why did DAS move from needle to scalpel cricothyroidotomy as first-line for CICO?
Needle technique requires jet ventilation (barotrauma risk), uses narrow-gauge cannula prone to kinking/dislodgement, and has a lower real-world success rate. The membrane is actually large enough (~9×30mm) for direct scalpel-bougie-tube placement, which is faster and more reliable once trained.
Q: Is cricothyroidotomy a definitive airway?
No — it's a rescue airway for the CICO emergency. Once oxygenation is secured, plan elective conversion to a formal surgical tracheostomy with ENT, as prolonged cricothyroidotomy carries higher risk of subglottic stenosis than a properly sited tracheostomy.
