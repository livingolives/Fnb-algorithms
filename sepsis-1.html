📖 Sepsis-3 Definitions (Singer et al., JAMA 2016)

Sepsis-3 removed SIRS criteria entirely. The focus shifted from the inflammatory response to organ dysfunction as the defining feature. A patient can have sepsis without meeting SIRS criteria, and SIRS alone does not constitute sepsis.
Sepsis
Life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by dysregulated host response to infection.

Criterion: Acute SOFA score increase ≥2 points above baseline.

Not just infection — organ dysfunction is the defining feature.
Septic Shock
Sepsis + both of:

① Vasopressor to maintain MAP ≥65 mmHg
② Lactate >2 mmol/L despite adequate fluid resuscitation.

28-day mortality >40%.
qSOFA — Bedside Screening Tool
Score ≥2 → suspect sepsis, escalate assessment. Not diagnostic — triggers full SOFA evaluation.

Respiratory rate ≥22/min (1 point)  ·  Altered mentation — GCS <15 (1 point)  ·  SBP ≤100 mmHg (1 point)

⏱ Hour-1 Bundle (SSC 2018, retained in 2026)

1
Measure Lactate
Initial lactate measurement. Remeasure if >2 mmol/L — confirms elevation is real, catches trajectory, and fulfils septic shock definition criterion post-fluids. Target normalisation to <2 mmol/L.
2
Blood Cultures Before Antibiotics
At least 2 sets (aerobic + anaerobic). Do not delay antibiotics >45 minutes waiting for cultures — but draw cultures first if possible.
3
Broad Spectrum Antibiotics
Within 1 hour of sepsis recognition. Every hour of delay in antibiotics increases mortality by ~7%. Cover likely pathogens based on source — don't wait for cultures.
4
30 ml/kg Crystalloid Bolus
For hypotension OR lactate ≥4 mmol/L. Balanced crystalloids preferred (Plasma-Lyte, RL) over 0.9% saline — hyperchloraemic acidosis risk. Reassess after each bolus — don't give blindly.
5
Vasopressors If Hypotensive
If MAP <65 during or after fluid resuscitation. Noradrenaline first line. Start early — don't wait to finish the full 30ml/kg if patient is profoundly hypotensive.

💉 Vasopressor Escalation in Septic Shock

Agent & Dose
Mechanism & Rationale
1
Noradrenaline
0.01–3 mcg/kg/min
α1 >> β1 → ↑SVR → ↑MAP. First line GRADE 1B. Maintains CO without excessive tachycardia.
2
Vasopressin
0.03 units/min (fixed)
V1 receptor → non-adrenergic vasoconstriction. Restores depleted endogenous vasopressin. Noradrenaline-sparing effect. VASST trial. Not weight-based.
3
Adrenaline
0.01–3 mcg/kg/min
α + β1 → ↑SVR + ↑CO. Add for refractory shock or when cardiac output support needed. Raises lactate independently — confounds monitoring.
4
Hydrocortisone
200 mg/day IV
Vasopressor-refractory shock — restores adrenoceptor sensitivity. ADRENAL trial. Not for all sepsis — only when >2 vasopressors failing.
Why vasopressin second (not more noradrenaline): Prolonged septic shock depletes endogenous ADH stores — relative vasopressin deficiency develops. Vasopressin acts on V1 receptors completely independent of catecholamine receptors — adding it restores a second vasoconstrictor pathway rather than saturating an already-stimulated α1 system. Allows noradrenaline dose reduction.

📈 Lactate-Guided Resuscitation

Lactate Clearance Target
Target: >10% reduction at 6 hours — validated as resuscitation endpoint.
Normalisation target: <2 mmol/L — clearance >10% is a good trend but not endpoint. Continue resuscitation until normalised.
LACTATE trial (Jones, JAMA 2010): Lactate clearance-guided resuscitation non-inferior to ScvO₂-guided therapy. Removed mandatory central line requirement from SSC bundle.
ScvO₂ trap in sepsis: High ScvO₂ (>70%) can indicate cellular dysoxia — mitochondrial dysfunction means O₂ cannot be utilised even when delivered. ScvO₂ normal ≠ adequate resuscitation in sepsis.
EGDT (Rivers, NEJM 2001): Original ScvO₂-guided protocol. Superseded by ARISE/ProCESS/ProMISe (2014–15) — EGDT not superior to usual care. ScvO₂ remains useful monitoring tool, not mandatory protocol.

🫁 VAP — Definition & Prevention Bundle

VAP definition: New or progressive pulmonary infiltrate on CXR + ≥2 of: fever >38.3°C, purulent secretions, WCC >12 or <4 × 10⁹/L, occurring >48 hours after intubation. CPIS score ≥6 supports diagnosis. Confirmed by BAL/quantitative cultures.
IHI BUNDLE 01
Head of Bed 30–45°
Reduces microaspiration of subglottic secretions. Continuous, not just during feeds.
IHI BUNDLE 02
Daily Sedation Vacation + SBT
Reduces ventilator days → reduces VAP exposure time. Spontaneous breathing trial daily.
IHI BUNDLE 03
Peptic Ulcer Prophylaxis
PPI or H2 blocker. Reduces stress ulceration — indirect VAP benefit via reduced aspiration of gastric acid.
IHI BUNDLE 04
DVT Prophylaxis
LMWH or compression devices. Reduces PE risk in ventilated patients.
IHI BUNDLE 05
Oral Chlorhexidine
0.12% CHX mouthwash. Reduces oropharyngeal colonisation → reduces microaspiration of pathogens.
ADD-ON
Subglottic Drainage + Cuff Pressure
Specialised ETT with subglottic suction port. Cuff pressure 20–30 cmH₂O — prevents microaspiration around cuff.
⚠ Routine ETT Change — Not Recommended
Change ETT at Day 7 routinely → No evidence base. Carries procedural risk. Not in any validated bundle.
Early tracheostomy at Day 7–10 — is evidence-based for patients requiring prolonged ventilation. Reduces sedation, ventilator days, potentially VAP. This is different from routine ETT swap.

🧬 NDM — New Delhi Metallo-beta-lactamase

⚠ Critical — Carbapenem Resistance
NDM is a carbapenemase — it hydrolyses and destroys the beta-lactam ring of ALL carbapenems. Meropenem will not work against NDM-positive organisms.
Meropenem for NDM Acinetobacter → Giving carbapenem to carbapenemase-producing organism = giving penicillin to MRSA.
Treatment options for NDM-positive organisms:

Ceftazidime-avibactam + Aztreonam — preferred combination. Avibactam inhibits NDM → restores aztreonam activity. Current first choice where available.

Colistin/Polymyxin B — last resort. Nephrotoxic, neurotoxic. Often only option in resource-limited settings.

Cefiderocol — newer siderophore cephalosporin. Active against NDM. Increasing use.

Fosfomycin — combination therapy adjunct.

Tigecycline — bacteriostatic, poor lung penetration. Adjunct only — not monotherapy for VAP.

🆕 SSC 2026 — Key Updates (Prescott et al., Crit Care Med 2026)

129 statements total — 46 new. Major changes from 2021:
SCREENING
Prehospital Screening
Standardised screening tools now recommended in prehospital setting. Early identification before hospital arrival explicitly encouraged.
RESPIRATORY
High-Flow Nasal Cannula First
HFNC now preferred initial device for sepsis-associated respiratory failure over standard O₂. Awake proning considered alongside HFNC.
VENTILATION
TV 6–8 ml/kg Without Lung Injury
Higher TV (6–8 ml/kg) now suggested in absence of lung injury — vs strict 6 ml/kg previously. Lung injury still requires 6 ml/kg IBW.
ANTIPYRETICS
Against Routine Antipyretics
New recommendation against routine antipyretics for improving clinical outcomes. May be used for symptom relief only — fever may be beneficial to host defence.
ADJUNCTS
Against Probiotics & Beta-Blockers
New recommendations against routine probiotics and beta-blockers for treatment of sepsis and septic shock.
POST-SEPSIS
Structured Follow-Up
New emphasis on transitions of care — post-discharge structured support, mental health services for survivors, pharmacist-led medication reconciliation.
What hasn't changed: Sepsis-3 definitions remain. Noradrenaline first-line vasopressor. Blood cultures before antibiotics. 30ml/kg crystalloid for hypotension/lactate ≥4. Lactate monitoring as resuscitation endpoint. Hour-1 bundle framework retained.

🎯 Examiner Pearls

Q: What did Sepsis-3 remove from the definition?
SIRS criteria entirely. Temperature, WCC, heart rate, respiratory rate — none of these are required for Sepsis-3 diagnosis. Organ dysfunction (SOFA ≥2) replaced the SIRS-based definition. A patient can have sepsis without SIRS, and SIRS without sepsis.
Q: ScvO₂ is 78% — is your septic shock patient adequately resuscitated?
Not necessarily. High ScvO₂ in sepsis can reflect cellular dysoxia — mitochondrial dysfunction means tissues cannot extract or utilise oxygen even when delivered. Normal ScvO₂ with persistently elevated lactate means the problem is at the cellular level, not the delivery level. Lactate trend is the better endpoint.
Q: Why does adrenaline confound lactate monitoring in septic shock?
Adrenaline stimulates β₂ adrenoceptors on skeletal muscle → accelerated aerobic glycolysis → increased lactate production independent of tissue hypoxia. A rising lactate on adrenaline infusion may be a drug effect, not worsening shock — making lactate clearance unreliable as a resuscitation endpoint when adrenaline is running.
Q: What is the vasopressin dose in septic shock and why is it not weight-based?
0.03 units/min fixed dose — not weight-based. Vasopressin in septic shock is used to restore depleted endogenous ADH (replacement dose), not as a titrated vasoconstrictor. The VASST trial used this fixed dose. Higher doses cause coronary and mesenteric vasoconstriction — the therapeutic window is narrow.
