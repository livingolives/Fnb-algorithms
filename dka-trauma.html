DKA in the Trauma Patient

Diagnosis · Management · Trauma-Specific Complexity
Scenario: 20yo with known/undiagnosed diabetes presents post-trauma with altered mentation, tachypnoea, dehydration. Could be DKA precipitated by the trauma (physiological stress), or a pre-existing DKA that caused the trauma (collapse → fall/RTA) — either direction is possible and changes your framing.

📊 Diagnostic Criteria

Parameter
Threshold
Glucose
>250 mg/dL (13.9 mmol/L) — can be lower if euglycaemic DKA (SGLT2 inhibitors, starvation, pregnancy)
pH
<7.3
Bicarbonate
<18 mmol/L
Ketones
Positive serum/urine ketones, or β-hydroxybutyrate >3 mmol/L
Anion gap
Elevated — high AG metabolic acidosis (ketoacids are the unmeasured anion)
Severity grading: Mild (pH 7.25-7.3), Moderate (pH 7.0-7.24), Severe (pH <7.0 or altered mental status/shock) — severity determines ICU vs ward-level management.

🎯 Management — Trauma-Adapted

1
Fluid Resuscitation First — Before Insulin
0.9% saline initially (isotonic), 1L in first hour then reassess. Trauma-specific conflict: if concurrent haemorrhagic shock, prioritise blood products/MTP per your DCR protocols — DKA fluid resuscitation does not override haemorrhage control. Correct volume depletion before starting insulin — insulin alone without fluid can worsen hypotension (shifts fluid intracellularly) and precipitates cerebral oedema.
2
Potassium — Check Before Insulin
DKA patients are total body K⁺ depleted despite potentially normal/high serum K⁺ (acidosis shifts K⁺ extracellularly). If K⁺ <3.3 mmol/L → replace potassium BEFORE starting insulin — insulin will drive K⁺ intracellularly and precipitate dangerous hypokalaemia/arrhythmia. If K⁺ 3.3-5.3, replace concurrently with insulin. If >5.3, withhold K⁺ initially, monitor.
3
Insulin — Fixed Rate IV Infusion
0.1 units/kg/hr IV infusion (not bolus — no evidence of benefit, may worsen hypokalaemia risk). Target glucose fall 50-70 mg/dL/hr. When glucose <250-300 mg/dL, add 5-10% dextrose to the fluid regimen and continue insulin (still needed to switch off ketogenesis, not just to lower glucose).
4
Bicarbonate — Rarely Indicated
Only consider if pH <6.9 with haemodynamic compromise. Routine bicarbonate use is not recommended — risk of paradoxical CNS acidosis, hypokalaemia, and (in children/adolescents) cerebral oedema.
5
Monitor for Cerebral Oedema
Rare but devastating, more common in younger patients. Avoid rapid osmotic shifts — correct glucose and sodium gradually. Headache, altered consciousness, or new focal neurology during treatment → stop and reassess, consider mannitol/hypertonic saline if genuine cerebral oedema develops (same principle as your TBI management).

⚠️ Trauma-Specific Complexity

Competing Priorities
Haemorrhage control takes priority — if the patient is also bleeding, DCR/MTP protocols run in parallel, not sequentially after DKA correction.
Lactate interpretation confounded — DKA causes an elevated anion gap independent of tissue hypoperfusion; use your Stewart/corrected AG approach to separate the ketoacid contribution from a genuine hypoperfusion-driven lactate rise.
Altered mental status attribution — is the GCS drop from DKA, TBI, or both? Don't anchor on one diagnosis; work up head injury independently per your TBI/C-spine protocols regardless of DKA severity.
Surgical timing — if surgery is needed, correct the acidosis and electrolytes as much as the clinical urgency allows first; DKA significantly increases perioperative risk (arrhythmia, hypotension on induction).

🎯 Examiner Pearls

Q: Why must you check potassium before starting insulin in DKA?
DKA patients are total-body potassium depleted despite normal or even high serum levels (acidosis shifts K⁺ extracellularly). Insulin drives K⁺ rapidly intracellularly — starting insulin in a patient with K⁺ <3.3 mmol/L can precipitate life-threatening hypokalaemia and arrhythmia. Replace K⁺ first if below this threshold.
Q: Trauma patient is both haemorrhaging and in DKA — which takes priority?
Haemorrhage control — activate MTP/DCR per standard trauma protocols. DKA fluid and insulin management runs alongside, not as a competing priority; permissive hypotension principles still apply for haemorrhage while correcting the metabolic derangement in parallel once major bleeding is controlled.
Q: Why avoid insulin bolus at initiation?
No evidence of faster resolution compared to infusion alone, and a bolus risks a more precipitous glucose/osmolality drop — increasing cerebral oedema risk, particularly relevant in younger patients. Fixed-rate IV infusion (0.1 units/kg/hr) is standard.
