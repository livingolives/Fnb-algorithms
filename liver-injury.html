Liver Injury Grading

AAST · WSES Classification · Grade IV Anaesthesia

📊 AAST Liver Injury Scale (I–VI)

Gr
Injury Description
I
Subcapsular haematoma <10% surface area OR capsular tear <1cm depth
II
Subcapsular haematoma 10–50% OR laceration 1–3cm depth
III
Subcapsular haematoma >50% OR laceration >3cm depth
IV
Laceration involving 25–75% of a hepatic lobe OR 1–3 Couinaud segments
V
Laceration >75% of lobe OR >3 segments within a lobe; OR juxtahepatic venous injury (retrohepatic vena cava/major hepatic veins)
VI
Hepatic avulsion — unsurvivable without immediate control
Grade V is the critical viva point: juxtahepatic venous injury (retrohepatic IVC/major hepatic veins) is the single most lethal liver injury pattern — catastrophic haemorrhage, may require atriocaval shunting.

⚖️ WSES — Physiology Trumps Anatomy

WSES (World Society of Emergency Surgery) reclassifies liver injury by haemodynamic status, not purely anatomical grade like AAST. The key conceptual shift: a Grade II injury in an unstable patient is managed more aggressively than a Grade IV injury in a stable patient.
WSES I — Minor
AAST I–II, haemodynamically stable
WSES II — Moderate
AAST III, stable
WSES III — Severe
AAST IV–V, stable
WSES IV — Severe + Unstable
ANY AAST grade, if haemodynamically unstable → mandates immediate operative management regardless of anatomical severity

🩸 Active Extravasation — Changes Everything

Scenario
Management
Stable, no blush
Non-operative management, serial observation
Stable + active blush
Angioembolisation — blush predicts NOM failure/re-bleeding regardless of current stability
Unstable, any grade
Operative management — WSES IV mandate
Key point: active contrast extravasation on CT mandates intervention even in a currently "stable" patient — it indicates ongoing arterial haemorrhage that will very likely progress. Don't be reassured by a normal blood pressure if the CT shows active bleeding.

💉 Anaesthetic Management — Grade IV/V Liver Injury

🩸Compounded Coagulopathy
The liver is both the site of injury AND the organ manufacturing clotting factors — a damaged liver cannot replace consumed factors as effectively. Unique vicious cycle not present in, e.g., splenic injury.
Anticipate more severe coagulopathy than blood loss alone predicts. TEG/ROTEM-guided factor replacement essential — target fibrinogen >1.5–2g/L, replace calcium and platelets proactively.
💊Drug Selection — Hepatic Independence
Atracurium (Hofmann elimination — organ-independent) over rocuronium/vecuronium (hepatic clearance)
Remifentanil (plasma esterase metabolism) over fentanyl/morphine (hepatic metabolism)
Matters less in a single acute trauma case than chronic liver failure, but still the correct principle to state
🔪Surgical Manoeuvres to Anticipate
Pringle manoeuvre — portal triad clamping to control inflow bleeding. Watch for haemodynamic changes on clamp/unclamp application and release.
Perihepatic packing — same damage control principles as abdominal/pelvic DCL. Anticipate staged return to theatre.
Retrohepatic caval injury (Grade V) — catastrophic haemorrhage; may require atriocaval shunting in extreme cases. Have massive transfusion capability ready before knife-to-skin.

🎯 Haemodynamic Targets

Permissive hypotension until haemostasis — target MAP ~65 mmHg, SBP 80–90 mmHg (not the 80–90 MAP used for TBI — don't conflate the two targets). If concurrent TBI present, CPP/MAP targets take priority over permissive hypotension — same conflict discussed in your emergency laparotomy page.
⚠ Examiner Traps
Confusing AAST (pure anatomy) with WSES (anatomy + physiology) — WSES is what actually drives the operative vs non-operative decision
Assuming a "stable" patient with active blush can be safely observed — active extravasation overrides current haemodynamic reassurance
Using TBI's MAP 80–90 target for a pure liver injury without TBI — liver trauma alone targets permissive hypotension, not high MAP

🎯 Examiner Pearls

Q: What does WSES add that AAST doesn't have?
Haemodynamic status as a classification dimension. WSES IV means "unstable" regardless of anatomical AAST grade, and mandates immediate operative management — physiology overrides anatomy in the decision to operate.
Q: Why is liver trauma coagulopathy worse than expected from blood loss alone?
The liver synthesises clotting factors. When it's the injured organ, its capacity to replace consumed factors is itself compromised — a vicious cycle unique to hepatic and to a lesser extent pancreatic trauma, not seen with splenic or renal injury.
Q: What is the most lethal pattern of liver injury and why?
Grade V juxtahepatic venous injury — involvement of the retrohepatic IVC or major hepatic veins. Massive, difficult-to-control haemorrhage from a location that's surgically challenging to access. May require atriocaval shunting as a last resort.
