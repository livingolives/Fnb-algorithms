⚡ CLASS II vs III — EXAM DISTINCTION
Class II: BP normal but pulse pressure narrowed (↑ diastolic from vasoconstriction). Compensated.

Class III: Systolic BP drops — vasoconstriction overcome. Decompensating. Requires blood products.

Key pearl: Pulse pressure narrows before systolic drops — earliest sign of significant haemorrhage.

💧 Response to Resuscitation

Rapid Responder
Responds and stays stable. Blood loss <20%. Continue monitoring. May not need MTP.
Transient Responder
Responds then deteriorates. Ongoing bleeding. Activate MTP. Prepare for surgery.
Non-Responder
No response. Exsanguinating. Immediate surgery / IR. In extremis — crash laparotomy.

🔄 Damage Control Resuscitation (DCR)

📉
Permissive Hypotension
SBP 80–90 mmHg until bleeding controlled.

TBI: MAP ≥80 mmHg.

Titrate to central pulse.
🩸
Haemostatic Resuscitation
1:1:1 pRBC:FFP:Platelets.

TXA 1g IV within 3h, then 1g over 8h.

Calcium replacement.
🔪
Damage Control Surgery
Abbreviated surgery. Control bleeding. Pack and close. Physiology first, anatomy later.
🚫 WHY NOT CRYSTALLOID FIRST?
Large crystalloid volumes → haemodilution → dilutes clotting factors → worsens coagulopathy.

Raising BP before haemorrhage control → "pops the clot" → re-bleeding → more hypotension → vicious cycle.

Crystalloid worsens the lethal triad. Blood products are the resuscitation fluid in haemorrhagic shock.

💀 Lethal Triad / Diamond of Death

🥶 Hypothermia
Impairs enzyme kinetics of coagulation cascade. <34°C = clinically significant coagulopathy. Target: >36°C.
🧪 Acidosis
↓ coagulation factor activity. ↓ platelet function. pH <7.1 = severe impairment. BD >6 = marker of shock.
🔬 Coagulopathy
ATC occurs within 30 mins of injury. Dilutional coagulopathy follows resuscitation. INR ≥1.5 = diagnostic.
➕ Diamond of Death (4th Element)
Hypocalcaemia — massive transfusion → citrate in blood products chelates ionised calcium → ↓ cardiac contractility + ↓ coagulation.

Give calcium chloride / calcium gluconate with every 4 units of pRBC in MTP.

🔴 Massive Transfusion Protocol (MTP)

TRIGGERS FOR MTP ACTIVATION
💉
Shock Index >1 (HR/SBP >1) — simple, rapid bedside marker
🩸
Predicted massive transfusion: >10 units pRBC in 24h or >4 units in 1h
📊
ABC Score ≥2: Penetrating mechanism, SBP ≤90, HR ≥120, FAST positive
🏥
Non-responder / transient responder to initial resuscitation
🔬
TEG/ROTEM: Evidence of coagulopathy on functional testing
MTP RATIO — 1:1:1
pRBC : FFP : Platelets = 1 : 1 : 1

Mimics whole blood. Maintains haemostatic potential.

+ TXA 1g IV within 3 hours of injury (CRASH-2 trial)
+ Calcium with every 4 units pRBC
+ Cryoprecipitate if fibrinogen <1.5 g/L
+ Warm all products — prevent hypothermia

Goal: Haemoglobin >7–8 g/dL, Platelets >50×10⁹/L, INR <1.5, Fibrinogen >2 g/L, iCa >1.1 mmol/L

🔬 Acute Traumatic Coagulopathy (ATC)

TRIGGER
Tissue injury + Systemic hypoperfusion — both required. Not injury alone.
↓
MECHANISM
Thrombomodulin expression ↑ on endothelium → Thrombin-Thrombomodulin complex → Protein C activation → inactivates Factors Va + VIIIa → ↓ thrombin generation + ↑ fibrinolysis
↓
RESULT
ATC within 30 minutes of injury — before resuscitation begins. Present in 25% of trauma admissions. 4× mortality increase.
↓
DIAGNOSIS
INR ≥1.5 or PT/aPTT prolonged >50% · Fibrinogen <1.5 g/L · Base deficit >6 mmol/L · TEG/ROTEM functional testing
↓
TREATMENT
1:1:1 MTP · TXA ≤3h · Correct lethal triad · TEG-guided product replacement · Fibrinogen/cryoprecipitate if low

📉 Permissive Hypotension — Targets

Blunt trauma (no TBI): SBP 80–90 mmHg · MAP ≥60 mmHg

Penetrating trauma: Titrate to central pulse only until OR

TBI + haemorrhage: MAP ≥80 mmHg — CPP must be preserved

Elderly / hypertensive: Higher baseline = higher target — "normal" for them may be SBP 120+

Endpoint: Surgical haemorrhage control — then normalise

💊 Tranexamic Acid (TXA) — CRASH-2

Mechanism: Antifibrinolytic — inhibits plasminogen activation → prevents clot breakdown

Dose: 1g IV over 10 min → then 1g IV over 8 hours

Timing: Within 3 hours of injury — efficacy declines sharply after 3h. After 3h = potential HARM (increased mortality in CRASH-2).

CRASH-2 trial: 20,211 trauma patients — TXA within 3h reduced all-cause mortality (RR 0.91). Benefit greatest within 1 hour.

Give TXA to ALL trauma patients with significant haemorrhage or at risk of haemorrhage — do not wait for coagulation results.

🔪 Haemorrhage Control — Source-Based

🩹
External: Direct pressure · Tourniquet (life-threatening limb haemorrhage) · Wound packing
🫁
Thoracic: Chest drain (haemothorax) · EDT if penetrating + <15 mins signs of life
🫃
Abdominal: FAST + → damage control laparotomy · Packing · Angioembolism
🦴
Pelvic: Pelvic binder → angioembolism → pre-peritoneal packing → REBOA Zone 3
🦵
Long bones: Splinting (femur = up to 1.5L hidden blood loss) · External fixation
🎯
REBOA: Resuscitative Endovascular Balloon Occlusion of Aorta — zones I/III for non-compressible haemorrhage

🎯 Examiner Pearls

Q: Why not vasopressors in haemorrhagic shock?
Haemorrhagic shock = volume problem. Vasopressors ↑ afterload on an empty ventricle → cardiovascular collapse. Fix volume first. Vasopressors only if haemorrhage controlled and hypotension persists.
Q: Crystalloid vs blood products — which first?
Blood products first in Class III/IV. Crystalloid dilutes clotting factors → worsens coagulopathy → lethal triad. Limited warm crystalloid acceptable as a bridge only (250–500ml aliquots).
Q: TBI + haemorrhagic shock — how do you manage?
The hardest scenario. TBI needs MAP ≥80 mmHg (CPP preservation). Haemorrhage needs permissive hypotension. Prioritise: secure haemorrhage control as fast as possible, then normalise BP for the brain. Avoid hypotension in TBI — doubles mortality.
Q: What is Shock Index and when is it useful?
Shock Index = HR ÷ SBP. Normal <1. SI >1 = significant haemorrhage. SI >1.4 = severe shock. Simple bedside calculation — no equipment needed. Useful in triage.
Q: What does EtCO₂ tell you in a trauma resus?
Low EtCO₂ (<20 mmHg) = poor cardiac output / severe haemorrhage. Correlates with metabolic acidosis and shock severity. Rising EtCO₂ during resuscitation = improving perfusion. A useful non-invasive resuscitation endpoint.
