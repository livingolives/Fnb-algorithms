⚡ Tension Pneumothorax
Most immediately fatal — diagnose clinically, treat before CXR!
→

Clinical Features:

▸Respiratory distress + hypoxia
▸Absent breath sounds unilaterally
▸Tracheal deviation AWAY from affected side
▸Engorged neck veins (raised JVP)
▸Hypotension + tachycardia
▸Hypoxia unresponsive to oxygen

Treatment:

1Needle decompression — 2nd ICS midclavicular line
2Follow with ICD at safety triangle (4th/5th ICS anterior axillary line)
3High flow O₂, secure airway if needed
💡 Examiner Pearl:

Tension PTX on ventilator → sudden ↑PIP, ↓SpO₂, ↓BP. NEVER wait for CXR — decompress immediately! In ATLS, diagnosis is clinical.

🩸 Massive Haemothorax
>1500ml blood in chest cavity
→

Definition:

▸>1500ml initial drain OR >200ml/hr for 4 hours

Clinical Features:

▸Absent breath sounds + dullness to percussion
▸Haemorrhagic shock signs
▸Flat neck veins (unlike tamponade!)

Treatment:

1Large bore ICD — safety triangle
2Activate MTP — 1:1:1 (pRBC:FFP:Platelets)
3Autotransfusion — collect drained blood → retransfuse!
4TXA 1g IV within 3 hours
5Thoracotomy if >1500ml or ongoing bleeding
💡 Examiner Pearl:

Autotransfusion gold standard — collected haemothorax blood = patient's own, type-matched, warm, contains clotting factors! Collect via ICD → infuse back. Saves lives in resource-limited settings!

💔 Cardiac Tamponade
Beck's Triad — classic presentation
→

Beck's Triad:

▸Hypotension
▸Muffled/distant heart sounds
▸Raised JVP (engorged neck veins)

Also Look For:

▸Pulsus paradoxus >10mmHg
▸POCUS — pericardial effusion + RV collapse

Treatment:

1Pericardiocentesis — xiphisternum → left shoulder, 45° angle
2Attach 3-way stopcock → drain + autotransfuse!
3Induction SITTING UP — positive pressure impedes venous return
4Surgeon scrubbed before induction — arrest at induction is common!
5Definitive: surgical pericardiotomy/window
💡 Examiner Pearl:

Tamponade + induction = catastrophic drop in BP. Positive pressure ventilation ↑ intrathoracic pressure → impedes venous return → no cardiac output. Always induce sitting up, surgeon scrubbed, knife on chest!

💨 Open Pneumothorax
"Sucking chest wound"
→

Mechanism:

▸Chest wall defect >2/3 tracheal diameter
▸Air preferentially enters via defect (less resistance)
▸Progressive respiratory failure

Treatment:

1THREE-SIDED occlusive dressing (flutter valve effect)
2NEVER fully occlude → converts to tension PTX!
3Definitive: ICD + surgical closure
💡 Examiner Pearl:

3-sided = top, left, right sealed. Bottom open = acts as one-way valve. Air exits on expiration, cannot re-enter. Full occlusion → tension PTX → death!

🦴 Flail Chest
≥3 ribs fractured in ≥2 places = free floating segment
→

Mechanism:

▸Paradoxical breathing — segment moves IN on inspiration
▸Underlying pulmonary contusion — the REAL killer!
▸Pain → splinting → atelectasis → hypoxia

Treatment:

1ANALGESIA first! — ESP block / thoracic epidural / PCA
2CPAP/NIV — pneumatic splinting of chest wall
3Intubation + ventilation if respiratory failure
4Surgical rib fixation if prolonged ventilation needed
💡 Examiner Pearl:

Flail chest diagnosis = CLINICAL. The paradoxical movement may not be obvious early (patient splinting). Underlying pulmonary contusion causes hypoxia — not the mechanical flail segment itself!

🚨 Airway Obstruction
Most immediately reversible killer
→

Causes in Thoracic Trauma:

▸Blood/secretions/teeth in airway
▸Laryngotracheal disruption
▸Tracheal compression from haematoma

Treatment:

1Suction + jaw thrust/chin lift
2RSI + intubation (video laryngoscopy)
3Surgical airway if cannot intubate
🔴 Traumatic Aortic Disruption
80% die at scene — suspect in high-speed deceleration
→

CXR Signs:

▸Widened mediastinum (>8cm)
▸Obliterated aortic knuckle
▸Tracheal deviation right
▸Left pleural cap

Management:

1CT angiography if haemodynamically stable
2REBOA Zone 1 if unstable — bridge to theatre
3Unstable → Emergency Dept Thoracotomy (EDT)
4Definitive: TEVAR (endovascular) or open repair
💡 Examiner Pearl:

Most common site = aortic isthmus (ligamentum arteriosum). Deceleration injury → shear force at fixed point. Pseudoaneurysm = contained rupture — can survive long enough to reach hospital!

💨 Tracheobronchial Injury
Rare but highly lethal — suspect persistent air leak
→

Clinical Features:

▸Persistent air leak despite ICD
▸Massive surgical emphysema
▸Tension PTX not responding to decompression
▸Haemoptysis

Management:

1Intubate distal to injury if possible
2Double lumen ETT / bronchial blocker for lung isolation
3Bronchoscopy for diagnosis + guide intubation
4Surgical repair
💗 Cardiac Contusion + Commotio Cordis
Blunt cardiac injury spectrum
→

Cardiac Contusion:

▸Bruising of myocardium — especially RV (anterior)
▸ECG: new RBBB, ST changes, arrhythmias
▸Troponin elevated
▸Management: monitoring, treat arrhythmias

Commotio Cordis:

▸Blunt impact during VULNERABLE T WAVE period
▸Triggers VF → cardiac arrest
▸NO structural cardiac damage!
▸Common: young athletes (cricket/baseball/hockey)
RxImmediate defibrillation — survival depends on speed!
💡 Examiner Pearl:

T wave = repolarisation = relative refractory period = electrically unstable. Small mechanical stimulus → re-entrant VF. "T for Trouble!" Defibrillate immediately — survival drops 10% per minute without defibrillation!

🫁 Oesophageal Rupture
Boerhaave's equivalent in trauma
→

Clinical Features:

▸Chest pain out of proportion to injury
▸Surgical emphysema
▸Pneumomediastinum on CXR
▸Left pleural effusion (saliva/gastric contents)
▸Rapidly deteriorating sepsis if delayed

Management:

1CT chest with contrast — confirms diagnosis
2Broad spectrum antibiotics immediately
3Surgical repair if <24hrs — stent if delayed
4NPO + NGT decompression
↕ Diaphragmatic Rupture
Commonly missed — left > right (liver protects right)
→

Clinical Features:

▸Bowel sounds in chest
▸NGT in chest on CXR — pathognomonic!
▸Respiratory distress from abdominal contents in chest
▸Often presents late — missed acutely

Management:

1DO NOT insert ICD — bowel in chest!
2NGT decompression
3Surgical repair — laparoscopic or open
💡 Examiner Pearl:

NGT coiling in left chest = diaphragmatic rupture until proven otherwise. Left side 90% because liver protects right hemidiaphragm from rupture. NEVER insert ICD blindly — you'll puncture bowel!

🫁 Pulmonary Contusion
Most common cause of death in chest trauma
→

Pathophysiology:

▸Alveolar haemorrhage + oedema → shunt → hypoxia
▸Worsens over 24-48hrs — NOT immediately apparent
▸CT more sensitive than CXR early
▸Often underlying flail chest injury

Management:

1Restrict IV fluids — worsens contusion!
2Aggressive analgesia — prevents splinting
3CPAP/NIV first line
4Lung protective ventilation if intubated

ABCDE Approach

A — Airway:

1Secure if GCS deteriorating or airway threatened
2RSI with MILS if c-spine not cleared
3Video laryngoscopy — preferred in trauma

B — Breathing:

1High flow O₂ — target SpO₂ >94%
2eFAST — pneumothorax + haemothorax
3ICD safety triangle for PTX/haemothorax

C — Circulation:

12x large bore IV + arterial line
2Activate MTP 1:1:1
3TXA 1g IV within 3 hours
4Calcium if iCa <1.1 mmol/L
5Autotransfusion from haemothorax
6Cell salvage — reduce allogeneic transfusion
7Warming — forced air blanket + warm fluids

Safety Triangle — ICD Insertion

▸Anterior border: lateral edge of pectoralis major
▸Posterior border: anterior edge of latissimus dorsi
▸Superior border: base of axilla
▸Level: 4th or 5th intercostal space
💡 Why Safety Triangle?

Avoids long thoracic nerve (latissimus), avoids breast tissue, avoids diaphragm (below 5th ICS anteriorly), avoids neurovascular bundle (stay above inferior rib margin)!

EDT vs REBOA — Decision

▸Unstable + no response to resus → EDT
▸Penetrating + witnessed cardiac arrest → EDT
▸Aortic injury + stable → REBOA Zone 1 → OT
▸Blunt + CPR >10 mins → futile (both)
