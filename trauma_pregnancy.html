Trauma in Pregnancy

ATLS 10th Ed · ALSO Guidelines · Core Concepts Critical Care 2026

🔬 Physiological Changes — Why Pregnancy Changes Everything

AIRWAY
Engorged mucosa — capillary engorgement → friable, bleeds easily on instrumentation. Use smaller ETT (6.5–7.0mm).
Pendulous breasts — impinge on laryngoscope handle. Use short-handled laryngoscope or CMAC.
Delayed gastric emptying — progesterone effect + pain → full stomach at any gestation. RSI mandatory.
↓ Lower oesophageal sphincter tone → aspiration risk greatly increased.

All pregnant patients = FULL STOMACH. RSI with cricoid pressure.
BREATHING — RAPID DESATURATION
↑ O₂ consumption — 20% increase (fetal + metabolic demands). Less oxygen reserve.
Basal atelectasis — gravid uterus splints diaphragm → ↓ FRC by 20%.
↑ RR + ↑ tidal volume — progesterone drives hyperventilation → PaCO₂ 28–32 mmHg is NORMAL in pregnancy.

Result: Pregnant patients desaturate faster than non-pregnant. Pre-oxygenation is critical. Apnoea tolerance ↓↓.

Ventilation target: PaCO₂ 28–32 mmHg (normal for pregnancy — do NOT over-ventilate to "normal" 40 mmHg as this is relatively hypercapnic for the fetus).
CIRCULATION — AORTOCAVAL COMPRESSION
Supine hypotension syndrome: Gravid uterus (>20 weeks) compresses IVC + aorta when supine → ↓ venous return → ↓ CO → hypotension.

Left lateral tilt 15–30° after 20 weeks — displaces uterus off IVC. If on spinal board: manual left uterine displacement (LUD).

Physiological anaemia: Plasma volume ↑ 40–50% but RBC mass ↑ only 20–30% → dilutional anaemia. Normal Hb in pregnancy = 10–12 g/dL.

↑ Blood volume: Pregnant women can lose up to 30–35% blood volume before showing signs of shock — fetus is already compromised while mother looks stable.

HR: Resting HR ↑ 15–20 bpm in pregnancy. Normal HR in pregnancy = 80–100 bpm.

BP: ↓ 10–15 mmHg in 2nd trimester (vasodilation), returns to normal by term.
HAEMATOLOGY + COAGULATION
Hypercoagulable state: ↑ Fibrinogen, ↑ Factors VII, VIII, X, XII. Protective against PPH — but ↑ DVT/PE risk.

Fibrinogen: Normal in pregnancy = 4–6 g/L (double non-pregnant). Fibrinogen is the FIRST factor to drop in obstetric haemorrhage/DIC.

Placental trauma → tPA release → hyperfibrinolysis. TXA indicated in obstetric haemorrhage (WOMAN trial).

Platelets: Slightly lower (gestational thrombocytopaenia) — normal down to 100×10⁹/L in pregnancy.

GFR: ↑ 50% in pregnancy → serum creatinine LOWER than normal. "Normal" creatinine (90 μmol/L) may indicate significant renal impairment in a pregnant patient.

👶 Fetal Considerations

Uteroplacental perfusion has NO autoregulation — entirely dependent on maternal cardiac output and MAP. Any maternal hypotension → immediate fetal compromise.

Fetal acidosis precedes maternal signs of shock — fetus is the canary in the coal mine.

Cardiotocography (CTG): Should be commenced as soon as possible (>20 weeks) — most sensitive indicator of fetal wellbeing and placental abruption.

Placental abruption: Most common cause of fetal death in trauma. May occur with apparently minor trauma. Uterus is rigid, tender. CTG: decelerations, loss of variability.

Resuscitate the mother → resuscitate the fetus. Fetal outcome follows maternal outcome.

🚨 Resuscitation — Key Modifications

AIRWAY
RSI mandatory (full stomach). Smaller ETT 6.5–7.0mm. CMAC/video laryngoscopy. Cricoid pressure. Have surgical airway ready — failed intubation more common in pregnancy.
↓
BREATHING
Pre-oxygenate aggressively. Target SpO₂ >95%. Ventilate to PaCO₂ 28–32 mmHg (normal for pregnancy). Avoid PaCO₂ 40 mmHg — relatively hypercapnic for fetus → umbilical vasoconstriction.
↓
CIRCULATION
Left lateral tilt 15–30° OR manual left uterine displacement >20 weeks. Maintain MAP — no permissive hypotension (uteroplacental has no autoregulation). IV access above diaphragm preferred (IVC compression). TXA if haemorrhage. MTP if massive haemorrhage.
↓
MONITORING
CTG as soon as possible >20 weeks. Obstetric review. FAST scan. Kleihauer-Betke test. Anti-D if Rh-negative. Foetal USS for placental assessment.

⚡ Perimortem Caesarean Section (PMCS)

THE 4-MINUTE RULE
Indication: Maternal cardiac arrest >20 weeks gestation.

Timeline:
· Start CPR immediately
· If no ROSC within 4 minutes → deliver baby
· Baby out by 5 minutes from arrest

Why it helps the MOTHER:
· Gravid uterus compresses IVC even during CPR → limits venous return → limits cardiac output from compressions
· Delivery relieves aortocaval compression → ↑ venous return → improves CPR effectiveness → better chance of maternal ROSC

Why it helps the BABY:
· Brain injury begins at 4–6 minutes of anoxia
· Delivery before 5 minutes → best neurological outcome

Gestation: >24 weeks = potentially viable fetus. 20–24 weeks = PMCS still beneficial for maternal resuscitation even if fetus non-viable.

Technique: Vertical midline incision. Classical uterine incision (vertical). No need for sterile prep — speed is everything. Continue CPR throughout.

🔬 Kleihauer-Betke (KB) Test

Purpose: Detect fetomaternal haemorrhage (FMH) — fetal RBCs crossing into maternal circulation.

Mechanism: Acid elution — HbF resists acid, maternal HbA dissolves. Fetal cells stain pink, maternal cells appear as ghosts. Count ratio → estimate volume of FMH.

Who needs it: All Rh-negative pregnant trauma patients. Also used in suspected placental abruption.

Anti-D immunoglobulin:
· Give within 72 hours to Rh-negative mothers
· Standard dose: 300 mcg covers 30 mL fetal blood
· If KB shows >30 mL FMH → calculate additional Anti-D dose

Limitation: KB test is semi-quantitative. Flow cytometry is more accurate but less widely available.

💊 Drug Considerations in Pregnancy

RSI drugs: Ketamine safe (1–2 mg/kg) — maintains BP, not teratogenic. Propofol safe. Thiopentone safe. Suxamethonium safe — quaternary structure, minimal placental transfer.

Rocuronium: Safe in pregnancy. Use sugammadex for reversal.

Neostigmine: Crosses placenta → fetal bradycardia, bronchoconstriction, increased secretions → potentially fetal asystole.
Use Atropine (NOT glycopyrrolate) concurrently — glycopyrrolate is quaternary → does NOT cross placenta → fetal bradycardia unprotected.

Vasopressors: Ephedrine — historically preferred (mixed α+β, maintains uteroplacental flow). Phenylephrine — now acceptable, evidence shows no worse fetal outcomes in spinal hypotension.

TXA: Safe in pregnancy (WOMAN trial). Give for obstetric haemorrhage within 3 hours.

Opioids: Cross placenta — neonatal respiratory depression. Have naloxone ready if delivery imminent.

NSAIDs: Avoid after 32 weeks — premature closure of ductus arteriosus.

🩸 Placental Abruption — Most Common Cause of Fetal Death

Mechanism: Shearing force separates placenta from uterine wall. Can occur with apparently minor trauma.

Signs: Uterine tenderness · Rigid abdomen · Vaginal bleeding (may be absent if concealed) · Uterine contractions · Fetal distress on CTG

CTG findings: Decelerations · Loss of variability · Sinusoidal pattern · Fetal tachycardia

Monitoring: CTG for minimum 4–6 hours after significant trauma even if initially normal — abruption can present late.

USS: Insensitive for abruption — do NOT use to exclude it. CTG is more sensitive.

Management: Resuscitate mother. Obstetric emergency → delivery if fetal distress or uncontrolled haemorrhage.

🎯 Examiner Pearls

Q: Why does permissive hypotension have no role in pregnant trauma patients?
Uteroplacental perfusion has no autoregulation — it is entirely pressure-dependent. Any maternal hypotension causes immediate reduction in placental blood flow → fetal hypoxia and acidosis. The fetus may be critically compromised while the mother appears haemodynamically reasonable. Maintain normal MAP throughout.
Q: Why use atropine and NOT glycopyrrolate with neostigmine in pregnancy?
Glycopyrrolate is a quaternary ammonium compound — does NOT cross the placenta. Neostigmine DOES cross the placenta → fetal bradycardia, bronchoconstriction, secretions. If glycopyrrolate is used, it cannot counteract the fetal muscarinic effects of neostigmine → fetal asystole risk. Atropine crosses the placenta → protects fetus from neostigmine effects. Use atropine.
Q: A pregnant trauma patient looks haemodynamically stable — can you be reassured?
NO. Pregnant patients can lose 30–35% of blood volume before showing haemodynamic compromise — due to increased blood volume of pregnancy. Meanwhile uteroplacental flow falls proportionally → fetal compromise occurs BEFORE maternal signs. Fetal CTG changes are the earliest warning. Always monitor the fetus.
Q: What is "normal" PaCO₂ in pregnancy and why does it matter on the ventilator?
Normal PaCO₂ in pregnancy = 28–32 mmHg (progesterone-driven hyperventilation). Ventilating to 40 mmHg (standard non-pregnant target) = relative hypercapnia for the pregnant patient → umbilical artery vasoconstriction → ↓ uteroplacental blood flow → fetal acidosis. Target PaCO₂ 28–32 mmHg on the ventilator.
