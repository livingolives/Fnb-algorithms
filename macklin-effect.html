Macklin Effect

Pneumomediastinum Without Airway/Oesophageal Rupture
The Macklin effect explains how pneumomediastinum can occur after blunt chest trauma without any visible tracheobronchial or oesophageal tear — a mechanism worth knowing because it prevents you from over-calling a major airway injury when the actual explanation is more benign.

🔬 Mechanism

Blunt chest trauma → sudden ↑ intra-alveolar pressure (Valsalva against a closed glottis, blast, or direct impact) relative to interstitial pressure
↓
Alveolar rupture at the base — air escapes into the bronchovascular sheath (perivascular interstitium)
↓
Air dissects centrally along the bronchovascular sheath, tracking toward the hilum — following the path of least resistance
↓
Air reaches the mediastinum → pneumomediastinum, and can further track into the neck (subcutaneous emphysema) or retroperitoneum — all without any macroscopic tear in the airway, lung, or oesophagus
✓ Clinical Significance
Pneumomediastinum from the Macklin effect is typically a benign, self-limiting finding.
It does NOT necessarily indicate tracheobronchial rupture or oesophageal perforation — both of which are far more serious and require urgent intervention.
Recognising this mechanism prevents unnecessary invasive workup (bronchoscopy, oesophagoscopy) in a patient who has pneumomediastinum but no other signs of major airway/oesophageal injury.

⚠️ When to Still Worry — Don't Anchor Purely on Macklin

Macklin effect is a diagnosis of exclusion — still actively rule out true airway/oesophageal injury if:

• Large volume/rapidly progressive pneumomediastinum
• Persistent large air leak from a chest drain despite lung re-expansion
• Haemoptysis, dysphagia, subcutaneous emphysema out of proportion to injury
• Mechanism strongly suggestive of direct tracheobronchial or oesophageal trauma (penetrating, severe deceleration)

In these situations, proceed to bronchoscopy/CT with contrast swallow as indicated — don't dismiss everything as benign Macklin effect.

🎯 Examiner Pearls

Q: A blunt trauma patient has pneumomediastinum on CT with no visible airway tear — is this always reassuring?
Usually yes if it fits the Macklin mechanism (alveolar rupture, air tracking along bronchovascular sheaths to the mediastinum) — but it's a diagnosis of exclusion. Persistent large air leak, worsening subcutaneous emphysema, haemoptysis, or dysphagia should still prompt bronchoscopy/oesophageal imaging to exclude true injury.
Q: Why does the air track toward the mediastinum specifically, rather than staying peripheral?
Air follows the path of least resistance along the perivascular/peribronchial interstitial planes (bronchovascular sheath), which converge centrally toward the hilum and mediastinum — this anatomical convergence is what channels the air centrally rather than it simply diffusing outward.
