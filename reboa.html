REBOA

Resuscitative Endovascular Balloon Occlusion of the Aorta

📖 Definition

REBOA = Resuscitative Endovascular Balloon Occlusion of the Aorta.

A balloon catheter inserted via the common femoral artery, advanced into the aorta, and inflated to partially or completely occlude aortic flow — increasing proximal perfusion pressure (heart + brain) while reducing haemorrhage distal to the balloon.

Purpose: Bridge to definitive haemorrhage control in OR/IR. Not a definitive treatment.

🗺️ Aortic Zones

REBOA ZONE ANATOMY + INDICATIONS
ZONE
I
Descending Thoracic Aorta
Left subclavian artery → Coeliac axis (T4–T12)
Non-compressible thoracic + upper abdominal haemorrhage. Liver, spleen, aortic injury. Haemodynamically unstable — bridge to OR.
MAX 30–60 MIN — ischaemia risk
ZONE
II
Visceral Aorta
Coeliac axis → Lowest renal artery (L1–L2)
NOT USED. Contains visceral vessels (SMA, renal arteries) — balloon inflation causes immediate mesenteric + renal ischaemia. High AKI and bowel infarction risk.
CONTRAINDICATED
ZONE
III
Infrarenal Aorta
Lowest renal artery → Aortic bifurcation (L2–L4)
Pelvic fracture haemorrhage. Junctional haemorrhage (groin). Non-compressible pelvic bleeding not controlled by pelvic binder.
MAX 60–90 MIN — lower ischaemia risk

⚡ REBOA vs EDT — Decision Framework

STABLE
Haemodynamically stable — BP responds and maintains with resuscitation.
→ CT scan → angioembolisation or elective OR. REBOA not indicated.
↓
UNSTABLE — REBOA ZONE
Haemodynamically unstable — BP not maintaining despite resuscitation. Still has pulse. Transient responder or non-responder.
→ REBOA — inflate balloon to restore proximal pressure → buy time → immediate OR for haemorrhage control.
↓
IN EXTREMIS — EDT ZONE
Cardiac arrest or perimortem — no pulse, CPR in progress.
Penetrating: witnessed arrest <15 mins · Blunt: CPR <5 mins
→ Emergency Department Thoracotomy (EDT) — open chest, cross-clamp aorta, internal cardiac massage, relieve tamponade.
Key distinction: REBOA needs a pulse. EDT is when REBOA is too late — patient is arresting.

REBOA sits between unstable and arrest. EDT is last resort when all else has failed.

⚖️ REBOA vs EDT — Head to Head

REBOA
Access: Femoral artery — percutaneous
Setting: ED, IR, even pre-hospital
Patient: Pulse present, unstable
Skill: Endovascular — lower threshold
Complications: Limb ischaemia, AKI, reperfusion injury
Time: Zone I ≤60 min, Zone III ≤90 min
EDT
Access: Left anterolateral thoracotomy
Setting: ED / OR only
Patient: Arrest / perimortem
Skill: Open surgery — higher threshold
Complications: Major — thoracic, cardiac injury
Survival: Penetrating 15–30% · Blunt <5%

✓ / ✗ Indications & Contraindications

INDICATIONS
· Non-compressible torso haemorrhage (NCTH) — chest, abdomen, pelvis
· Haemodynamically unstable (SBP <90 despite resuscitation)
· Pelvic fracture with haemodynamic instability — Zone III
· Bridge to OR when surgical team not immediately available
· Junctional haemorrhage not amenable to tourniquet — Zone III
CONTRAINDICATIONS / CAUTION
· Cardiac arrest — EDT preferred
· Aortic injury at intended zone — balloon inflation worsens injury
· Zone II — NEVER (visceral ischaemia)
· TBI — use with extreme caution: ↑ proximal BP may ↑ ICP
· Femoral artery injury / thrombosis — no access
· Coagulopathy uncorrected — address with MTP concurrently

⚠️ Reperfusion Injury — After Balloon Deflation

Balloon deflation after prolonged inflation → sudden restoration of blood flow to ischaemic territory → reperfusion injury:

· Massive systemic inflammatory response
· Hyperkalaemia (from ischaemic cells)
· Lactic acidosis surge
· Myocardial depression
· AKI — especially after Zone I (renal ischaemia)

Mitigate by: Partial deflation (not sudden complete) · Ensure haemorrhage controlled before deflating · Calcium ready · Bicarbonate if severe acidosis · ICU post-op

🎯 Examiner Pearls

Q: Why is Zone II never used?
Zone II contains the coeliac axis, SMA and renal arteries. Balloon inflation at this level immediately occludes these vessels → mesenteric ischaemia + bowel infarction + bilateral AKI. The risk far outweighs any benefit. Zone I or Zone III only.
Q: Why is time limit shorter for Zone I than Zone III?
Zone I occludes the descending thoracic aorta — cuts off blood supply to spinal cord (anterior spinal artery), kidneys, bowel. Prolonged occlusion causes irreversible spinal cord ischaemia (paraplegia) and multi-organ failure. Zone III only occludes infrarenal aorta — lower ischaemia burden on critical organs.
Q: REBOA vs resuscitative thoracotomy — which in blunt trauma arrest?
Blunt trauma arrest has dismal outcomes with EDT (<5% survival). REBOA can be considered in blunt trauma with witnessed arrest <5 mins as a less invasive alternative. However evidence remains limited — current guidelines favour EDT for arrest in penetrating trauma. Blunt arrest = very select cases only.
Q: How do you confirm correct balloon position?
Fluoroscopy is gold standard — confirms zone placement radiologically. CXR can confirm Zone I (balloon should be at T4–T12, below carina). Pressure waveform change on inflation confirms occlusion. Point-of-care ultrasound can visualise balloon in aorta. Avoid blind inflation without confirmation where possible.
