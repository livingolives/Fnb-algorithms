ESP Block vs Thoracic Epidural

Rib Fracture Analgesia · BLUE Protocol Worked Case

🧬 Anatomical Target — Erector Spinae Plane

Paraspinal Cross-Section — T5 Level
Injection deep to erector spinae muscle, superficial to the transverse process (TP). LA tracks through costotransverse foramina to the paravertebral space, reaching ventral rami (intercostal nerves — motor to intercostals + sensory to chest wall) — this is what relieves rib fracture pain and reduces splinting.

⚠️ Common Confusion — Root vs Ramus

Inside Spinal Cord/Foramen
Dorsal root = sensory only
Ventral root = motor only
These fuse inside the foramen
After Exiting Foramen
Dorsal ramus = mixed — paraspinal muscles + back skin
Ventral ramus = mixed — intercostal muscles + chest wall skin
Both carry motor AND sensory fibres
The dorsal=sensory/ventral=motor rule applies only to roots inside the spinal canal. Once they fuse into the mixed spinal nerve and split into rami, both rami are mixed nerves — the distinction becomes anatomical territory (back vs chest wall), not function.

🚫 Practical Barriers to Epidural in Acute Trauma

Coagulopathy
ATC, anticoagulant use, post-MTP — very common in trauma. Epidural haematoma risk unacceptable.
Haemodynamic instability
Sympathectomy on top of unrecognised hypovolaemia can precipitate cardiovascular collapse.
Inability to position
Acutely painful, distressed, or intoxicated patients often cannot sit/flex the spine.
Unclear spinal status
Spinal injury not yet excluded makes neuraxial placement risky until cleared.

📋 Worked Case — What ESP Block Does NOT Fix

55M · Unilateral Rib Fractures 4–9 · ESP Block at T5 Performed
Pain score2/10 — good
SpO₂ at 6h93% RA
RRStill elevated
Key insight: good analgesia does NOT guarantee good oxygenation. ESP block controls pain and reduces splinting-related hypoventilation — but does NOT treat a mechanical (pneumothorax) or parenchymal (pulmonary contusion) cause of hypoxia. Persistent hypoxia despite good pain scores means you must look beyond the block.
Two differentials to consider:

1. Pneumothorax — either from the original rib fracture (bony spike lacerating pleura) or procedure-related (rare with ESP given the plane is superficial to pleura, but recognised).

2. Pulmonary contusion — alveolar flooding by inflammatory fluid/blood from the underlying lung injury → V/Q mismatch → hypoxia independent of pain control. Often worsens over 24–48h — hypoxia at 6h may reflect evolving contusion, not block failure.

🔵 BLUE Protocol — Bedside Lung Ultrasound in Emergency

Developed by Lichtenstein for undifferentiated acute respiratory failure — not trauma-specific, but the same sonographic signs apply directly to trauma chest injury assessment. Rapid, bedside, radiation-free — ideal next step in this exact scenario.
Absent Lung Sliding
Pleural layers not moving against each other — suggests pneumothorax
Barcode / Stratosphere Sign
M-mode — straight horizontal lines instead of granular pattern. Confirms pneumothorax
Seashore Sign
M-mode — granular pattern below pleural line. Normal, excludes PTX at that point
Comet-Tail / B-Lines
Vertical artefacts from pleural line — interstitial fluid/oedema, consistent with contusion
Subpleural Consolidation
Hypoechoic, tissue-like pattern with air bronchograms — supports pulmonary contusion/consolidation
1
Clinical Reassessment
Reduced air entry + hyperresonance → pneumothorax pattern. Crepitations → contusion pattern.
2
Lung Ultrasound (BLUE Protocol)
Check for lung sliding, M-mode signs, B-lines, subpleural consolidation as above.
3
ABG + CXR
Assess PaO₂/FiO₂ ratio, degree of shunt. Confirm findings radiographically if time allows.
4
Management Branch
Pneumothorax → chest drain.
Contusion → supportive: lung-protective ventilation if intubation needed, judicious fluids (avoid over-resuscitation — worsens contusion oedema), consider HFNC/CPAP to avoid intubation, physiotherapy, continue the ESP block to enable effective coughing and secretion clearance.

🎯 Examiner Pearls

Q: ESP block gives good pain relief but patient is still hypoxic — has the block failed?
No — the block is working exactly as intended (analgesia). Persistent hypoxia despite good pain control means a separate mechanical or parenchymal problem exists — pneumothorax or pulmonary contusion. Don't conflate analgesic success with respiratory adequacy; they are two different endpoints.
Q: Why might pulmonary contusion hypoxia worsen over the first 24-48 hours despite good analgesia?
Contusion is an evolving injury — ongoing alveolar haemorrhage and inflammatory oedema accumulate over the first 1-2 days, independent of pain control. This is why serial reassessment (not a single check) matters in rib fracture patients.
Q: Level selection for ESP block — how do you choose?
Target the midpoint of the fractured rib segment, or the level of maximal tenderness. For ribs 4–9, that's typically T5. Identify the spinous process at that level as your midline/level marker, then move needle entry 2–3cm lateral to reach the transverse process — your actual target, injecting just superficial to it.
