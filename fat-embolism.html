Pathognomonic
Timing: Symptoms appear 24–72 hours after long bone or pelvic fracture. Early presentation (<12h) is thromboembolic; delayed presentation (days) reflects biochemical/inflammatory cascade. Incidence: 1–3% of isolated long bone fractures, up to 10% with bilateral femoral fractures.

⏱ Pathophysiological Timeline

Hours 1–12
Mechanical Phase

24–72h

Serum lipase hydrolyses fat → free fatty acids + triglycerides → oxidative stress → endothelial injury → ARDS, coagulopathy, DIC
Days
Inflammatory Phase
DAMPs release → systemic inflammatory response → multi-organ dysfunction. Cerebral fat emboli → neurological deterioration independent of hypoxia

🔬 Pathophysiology — Two Theories

Theory 1 — Mechanical
Long bone fracture → fat droplets mobilised from marrow
Enter venous circulation → reach pulmonary vasculature
Occlude small pulmonary vessels → V/Q mismatch
Pulmonary HTN → right heart strain → haemodynamic compromise
Paradoxical emboli through PFO → cerebral/systemic
Theory 2 — Biochemical
Serum lipase activated → hydrolyses fat emboli in situ
Free fatty acids + triglycerides released locally
Oxidative stress → reactive oxygen species
Endothelial injury → capillary leak → pulmonary oedema
Procoagulant/inflammatory cascade → ARDS, DIC
Reality: Both mechanisms operate simultaneously. The mechanical phase explains early haemodynamic compromise; the biochemical phase explains the delayed ARDS picture and why severity is disproportionate to the embolic burden alone.

🎯 Management

FES-Specific — Most Important
Early fracture fixation — the single most important intervention. Reaming and fixation stops ongoing fat embolisation from the fracture site. Every hour of delay = continued embolisation.
Corticosteroids — controversial. Methylprednisolone may reduce severity if given prophylactically in high-risk patients (bilateral femoral fractures). Not standard of care but referenced in exam context.
01
Lung Protective Ventilation
TV 6 ml/kg IBW · Plateau pressure <30 cmH₂O · Driving pressure <15 cmH₂O · PEEP titrated individually · Permissive hypercapnia (PaCO₂ up to 55–60)
02
Prone Positioning
PaO₂/FiO₂ <150 despite optimised PEEP for >12h → prone for ≥16 hours/session. PROSEVA trial (Guérin, NEJM 2013): 28-day mortality 32% → 16%.
03
Neuromuscular Blockade
Cisatracurium for refractory dyssynchrony or hypoxaemia. Not routine for all ARDS (ROSE trial questioned routine use). Use for specific indications.
04
ECMO
Rescue therapy when conventional ventilation fails. PaO₂/FiO₂ <80 despite optimal settings for >6h. Refer to ECMO-capable centre early — don't wait until unsalvageable.

🎯 Examiner Pearls

Q: What is Purtscher's retinopathy?
Fundoscopic finding in FES — fat emboli visible in retinal vessels, with cotton-wool spots and flame haemorrhages. Pathognomonic of fat embolism reaching the systemic circulation via paradoxical embolism through a patent foramen ovale or overwhelmed pulmonary filtration. Classic viva trap — always mention this when listing minor criteria.
Q: PF ratio is 120. Does this meet Berlin ARDS criteria and what severity?
Yes — bilateral infiltrates + PF <200 + non-cardiac cause + onset within 1 week. Severe ARDS (Berlin: mild 200–300, moderate 100–200, severe <100). Triggers proning protocol.
Q: Why does early fracture fixation reduce FES severity?
The fracture site is the ongoing source of fat embolisation. Intact marrow under pressure continuously releases fat into the venous circulation. Reaming and fixation eliminates the source — mechanical containment stops embolisation regardless of what's already in circulation.
Q: Driving pressure vs plateau pressure — which is more predictive of mortality in ARDS?
Driving pressure (= plateau pressure − PEEP). Amato et al. (NEJM 2015) showed driving pressure >15 cmH₂O independently associated with mortality even when TV and plateau were within "safe" limits. Current target: driving pressure <15 cmH₂O, preferably <13.
