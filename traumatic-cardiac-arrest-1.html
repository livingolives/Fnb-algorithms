⚡ TCA vs Medical Cardiac Arrest

TCA is a mechanically caused arrest — the heart is structurally or haemodynamically prevented from functioning. External CPR alone generates negligible output in an empty, hypovolaemic, or obstructed heart. The priority is fixing the cause, not compressing the chest.

🎯 Reversible Causes — HITS Mnemonic

H
Hypovolaemia
Haemorrhage → empty ventricle → no output regardless of rhythm
Haemorrhage control (tourniquet, EDT, REBOA) · Massive transfusion · permissive hypotension once ROSC
I
↑ Intrathoracic Pressure
Tension pneumothorax → mediastinal shift → IVC compression → cardiac arrest
Bilateral finger thoracostomies within 10 seconds — do NOT wait for CXR
T
Tamponade
Pericardial collection → diastolic compression → obstructive shock → arrest
Pericardiocentesis (bridge) or EDT pericardiotomy (definitive)
S
Severe Hypoxia
Airway obstruction, haemopneumothorax, flail chest → hypoxic arrest
Definitive airway (RSI/surgical) · ventilate with 100% O₂ · drain haemopneumothorax
Order of priorities in TCA: Airway → bilateral thoracostomies → haemorrhage control → EDT if indicated. CPR continues throughout but does not precede reversible cause treatment. Address all four simultaneously where possible — most TCA has multiple contributing causes.

🔪 EDT — What You Achieve (4 Goals)

1
Relieve Cardiac Tamponade
Anterolateral thoracotomy → pericardiotomy (incise anterior to phrenic nerve, longitudinally) → evacuate clot → immediate decompression of obstructive shock.
2
Open Cardiac Massage
Generates 3× better cardiac output than external CPR. Two-handed or single-handed biventricular compression. Allows direct assessment of cardiac activity and filling.
3
Control Intrathoracic Haemorrhage
Direct pressure, vascular clamping, cardiac wound repair. Hilar cross-clamp for massive pulmonary haemorrhage (twist lung on its hilum).
4
Descending Aortic Cross-Clamp
Redistributes blood to coronary and cerebral circulation. Reduces subdiaphragmatic haemorrhage. Maximum safe time: <30 minutes. Beyond 30–60 min → spinal cord ischaemia via artery of Adamkiewicz (T9–T12). Target: clamp as bridge to theatre, release as soon as haemorrhage controlled.

🫀 Cardiac Wound Repair — Priority Ladder

1
Finger Occlusion
Immediate — thumb or finger directly over laceration. Stops bleeding, buys time, no tools required.
2
Horizontal Mattress Sutures
2-0 or 3-0 prolene. Primary repair. Sutures placed parallel to coronary vessels to avoid inadvertent ligation.
3
Skin Staples
Rapid temporary closure if prolene unavailable. Genuine damage control option. Bridge to OR.
4
Foley Catheter Balloon
For larger or irregular lacerations — insert through wound, inflate balloon, apply traction to tamponade from inside. Effective bridge for complex injuries.
Why RV is more forgiving than LV:

Wall thickness: RV ~3–4mm vs LV ~8–11mm. Sutures hold better in thicker muscle — LV repairs are technically harder.

Pressure: RV systolic ~25 mmHg vs LV ~120 mmHg. RV suture line faces 5× less hydraulic stress per systole — far lower dehiscence risk, more time before exsanguination, better survival.

⚠️ Examiner Traps

Don't fall for these
CPR first in penetrating TCA → Fix the cause first — bilateral thoracostomies, airway, EDT. CPR continues alongside but does not take priority.
Commotio cordis is a reversible cause of TCA → It's a cause of arrest, not a treatable cause during resuscitation. HITS: Hypovolaemia, ↑Intrathoracic pressure, Tamponade, Severe hypoxia.
EDT in blunt TCA with no signs of life → Survival essentially zero. ATLS/EAST guidelines advise against. Don't crack the chest for blunt arrest.
Aortic cross-clamp is safe up to 60 minutes → Target <30 minutes. 30–60 is the absolute outer limit. >60 minutes → near-certain paraplegia.
Adrenaline 1mg as in ACLS → Avoid or use sparingly in TCA — worsens vasoconstriction in hypovolaemia, increases myocardial oxygen demand on a stunned heart.

🎯 Examiner Pearls

Q: Why is external CPR ineffective in haemorrhagic TCA?
External compressions generate cardiac output by compressing a blood-filled ventricle. In hypovolaemic arrest, the ventricle is empty — no blood to eject. Compressions also cannot address the underlying cause (haemorrhage, tamponade, tension PTX). Fixing the mechanism is the only intervention that can restore output.
Q: What is the artery of Adamkiewicz and why does it matter in aortic cross-clamping?
The arteria radicularis magna — the dominant blood supply to the lower two-thirds of the spinal cord. Arises from the aorta at T9–T12 in most patients. Descending aortic cross-clamp occludes it → anterior spinal artery syndrome → paraplegia with preserved posterior column function. Maximum safe clamp time <30 minutes to minimise this risk.
Q: Signs of life in TCA — name them all.
Pupillary response · spontaneous movement · organised cardiac activity on monitor · detectable pulse · measurable BP. Even one sign present = signs of life present = EDT indicated within mechanism-specific downtime limits.
Q: When do you cross-clamp the hilum rather than the descending aorta in EDT?
Massive pulmonary haemorrhage — when the source is the lung rather than systemic. Hilar twist (rotating the lung 180° on its hilum) or hilar clamp controls both pulmonary arterial and venous bleeding simultaneously. Descending aortic cross-clamp does not control pulmonary haemorrhage.
