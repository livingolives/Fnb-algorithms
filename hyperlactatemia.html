Hyperlactataemia

Lactate Physiology & Monitoring in Trauma

📖 Definition & Thresholds

Lactate is the end-product of anaerobic glycolysis — pyruvate is reduced to lactate by lactate dehydrogenase when mitochondrial oxidative metabolism cannot keep pace with pyruvate production. It is cleared mainly by the liver (Cori cycle) and, to a lesser extent, the kidneys.

🧬 Cohen–Woods Classification (1976)

Type A
MechanismClinically evident tissue hypoxia / hypoperfusion → forced anaerobic glycolysis
CausesAll shock states — hypovolaemic, cardiogenic, obstructive, septic · severe hypoxaemia · regional ischaemia (limb/mesenteric) · seizures · strenuous exercise
Type B
B1 · DiseaseLiver disease, malignancy, diabetes, renal failure, thiamine deficiency
B2 · Drugs/toxinsMetformin/biguanides, isoniazid, propylene glycol, β-agonists, propofol, linezolid, NRTIs, cyanide
B3 · Inborn errorMitochondrial myopathies, congenital enzyme defects
Discriminator — lactate:pyruvate ratio. Elevated L:P ratio → true tissue hypoxia driving anaerobic glycolysis (Type A). Normal L:P ratio with high lactate → impaired oxidative metabolism without hypoxia (Type B).

⚠️ Common Mix-Ups (Examiner Traps)

Check yourself before the viva
It's Cohen–Woods, not "Wood & Cohen" — Cohen RD & Woods HF, 1976.
Mannitol is not a Type B cause — it causes a metabolic alkalosis. The classic biguanide is metformin.
Hyperlactataemia begins at >2 mmol/L, not 6 — don't shift the cut-off.
Septic shock is filed under Type A by convention, but mechanistically it's mixed — catecholamine-driven aerobic glycolysis and impaired hepatic clearance (Type B mechanisms) contribute alongside any true hypoperfusion.

🎯 Role of Lactate Monitoring in Trauma

ON ARRIVAL
Admission lactate is an independent prognostic marker — higher initial values track with injury severity and mortality risk.
SERIAL — 6H / 12H / 24H
Trend matters more than a single value. Time to lactate clearance/normalisation correlates strongly with survival (Abramson et al., J Trauma 1993).
RESUSCITATION ENDPOINT
Used alongside base deficit to titrate ongoing resuscitation — failure to clear suggests inadequate volume, ongoing haemorrhage, or both.
OCCULT HYPOPERFUSION
HR/BP can normalise while lactate stays elevated — this discordance flags ongoing tissue hypoperfusion masked by compensated vitals, and should prompt further resuscitation/work-up rather than reassurance.

🎯 Examiner Pearls

Q: Vitals normalise but 6h lactate is still 3.5 mmol/L — what does this tell you?
Occult hypoperfusion. Normal HR/BP do not guarantee adequate tissue perfusion — compensatory mechanisms can mask ongoing shock. A non-clearing lactate should trigger reassessment for occult haemorrhage and continued targeted resuscitation, not reassurance.
Q: Is sepsis-related hyperlactataemia always due to tissue hypoxia?
No. While conventionally filed under Type A, septic shock lactate rise is multifactorial: true hypoperfusion in some tissue beds, plus catecholamine-driven accelerated aerobic glycolysis and reduced hepatic clearance — Type B-like mechanisms operating in parallel.
Q: What lactate threshold features in the Sepsis-3 definition of septic shock?
Lactate >2 mmol/L despite adequate fluid resuscitation, combined with the need for vasopressors to maintain MAP ≥65 mmHg (Singer et al., JAMA 2016).
Q: How would you distinguish Type A from Type B lactic acidosis at the bedside?
Clinical context first — is there evidence of shock/hypoxaemia? Then biochemically, an elevated lactate:pyruvate ratio supports Type A; a normal ratio with high lactate points to Type B (drug, disease, or inborn error driving production/clearance independent of oxygen delivery).
