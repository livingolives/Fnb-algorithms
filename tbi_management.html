TBI Management

BTF 4th Ed · SIBICC Protocol · Core Concepts Critical Care 2026

⚖️ Monroe-Kellie Doctrine

CRANIUM = RIGID, FIXED VOLUME
Vbrain + VCSF + Vblood = CONSTANT
Any ↑ in one compartment must be compensated by ↓ in another.

Stage 1 — Compensation: CSF displaced to spinal canal · Venous blood squeezed out → ICP maintained

Stage 2 — Decompensation: Compensatory reserve exhausted → sharp exponential ↑ ICP → ↓ CPP → ischaemia → cytotoxic oedema → more ICP rise → herniation
CPP = MAP − ICP
Target CPP: 60–70 mmHg (BTF 4th Ed)
>70 mmHg → risk of ARDS from aggressive fluid/pressor use
<50 mmHg → cerebral ischaemia

⚠️ Secondary Brain Insults — Prevent These

HYPOTENSION
SBP >90 mmHg
MAP ≥80 mmHg
Single episode SBP <90 mmHg doubles mortality. Most modifiable killer.
HYPOXIA
SpO₂ ≥94%
PaO₂ >60 mmHg
Hypoxia + hypotension together = catastrophic secondary injury.
HYPERCAPNIA
PaCO₂ 35–40 mmHg
↑CO₂ → cerebral vasodilation → ↑CBF → ↑ICP.
HYPOCAPNIA
PaCO₂ >30 mmHg
↓CO₂ → vasoconstriction → ↓CBF → ischaemia. Prophylactic hyperventilation HARMFUL.
HYPERTHERMIA
Temp <38°C
↑ metabolic rate → ↑ O₂ demand → worsens ischaemia. Actively cool.
HYPO/HYPERGLYCAEMIA
Glucose 6–10 mmol/L
Hypoglycaemia = direct secondary insult. Tight control (4–6) causes dangerous episodes.
HYPONATRAEMIA
Na⁺ 140–155 mmol/L
Hyponatraemia → cerebral oedema → ↑ICP. Target mild hypernatraemia.
ANAEMIA
Hb >7–8 g/dL
(some guidelines >10 in TBI)
↓ O₂ carrying capacity → ↓ cerebral O₂ delivery → secondary ischaemia.

🚨 Initial Management — ABCDE

A — AIRWAY
GCS ≤8 = intubate. C-spine protection (MILS). RSI: ketamine 1–2 mg/kg (does NOT raise ICP when ventilated — safe, haemodynamically favourable) + rocuronium 1.2 mg/kg. CMAC / video laryngoscopy. Confirm with EtCO₂.
↓
B — BREATHING
Target PaCO₂ 35–40 mmHg. SpO₂ ≥94%. Avoid hypocapnia (vasoconstriction → ischaemia). Brief hyperventilation (PaCO₂ 30–35) only for acute herniation as bridge to surgery — NOT prophylactic.
↓
C — CIRCULATION
MAP ≥80 mmHg in TBI + haemorrhage. Avoid hypotension. Isotonic/hypertonic fluids — NOT hypotonic (0.45% NaCl worsens cerebral oedema). Vasopressors if volume insufficient: noradrenaline first line. NO permissive hypotension.
↓
D — DISABILITY
GCS, pupils, lateralising signs. Unilateral dilated sluggish pupil = CN III compression = uncal herniation. Bilateral fixed = late sign. Glucose check — treat hypoglycaemia immediately. CT head after airway secured.
↓
E + POSITIONING
Head up 30°, midline — optimises venous drainage. Avoid tight ETT ties (obstruct JVD). Log roll. Avoid hyperthermia. Keep Na⁺ 140–155.

📊 ICP Monitoring — Indications (BTF 4th Ed)

Indicated (Level II): GCS ≤8 + abnormal CT (haematoma, contusion, swelling, compressed basal cisterns, midline shift)

Indicated (Level III): GCS ≤8 + normal CT if 2 or more of: age >40 · motor posturing · SBP <90 mmHg

Consider also: GCS >8 with large/multiple contusions at high risk of progression · Pre-op for extracranial surgery where exam limited

Gold standard device: External Ventricular Drain (EVD) — measures ICP AND drains CSF therapeutically

Treat ICP at: >20–22 mmHg sustained >5 minutes

📈 ICP Management — Tiered Approach (SIBICC)

TIER ZERO — UNIVERSAL
Basic ICU Care
Head 30° elevated, midline · Adequate sedation + analgesia · Normothermia · Normoglycaemia · Normonatraemia (Na 140–155) · Avoid hypoxia · Maintain CPP 60–70 mmHg · Haemoglobin >7 g/dL · Seizure prophylaxis (levetiracetam — NOT valproate) · ETT ties not obstructing JVD · Nutrition within 72h
TIER ONE
First Line ICP Lowering
CSF drainage via EVD (if in situ)
Mannitol 0.25–1 g/kg IV bolus — osmotic diuretic, reduces cerebral oedema. Monitor serum osmolality <320 mOsm/L
Hypertonic saline 3% or 23.4% — raises Na⁺, draws water from brain. Preferred in hypotensive TBI (no diuretic effect)
↑ sedation · ↓ metabolic demand · PaCO₂ low-normal end (35 mmHg)
TIER TWO
Second Line
Mild hyperventilation PaCO₂ 32–35 mmHg (not <30)
Neuromuscular paralysis (if adequate sedation not reducing ICP)
MAP Challenge — assess cerebrovascular autoregulation. If intact: ↑MAP → ↓ICP. If disrupted: ↑MAP → ↑ICP
Adjust CPP target based on autoregulation status
TIER THREE — REFRACTORY
Last Resort
Barbiturate coma — pentobarbital or thiopentone. Titrate to ICP control. Avoid if haemodynamically unstable. Monitor EEG for burst suppression.
Decompressive craniectomy — DECRA trial: reduces ICP but may worsen functional outcome. RESCUE-ICP: reduces mortality but ↑ vegetative survival.
Mild hypothermia 35–36°C — EUROTHERM trial: 33°C caused HARM. If used: 35–36°C only, for refractory ICP.

💨 Hyperventilation — The Trap

⚠️ DO NOT HYPERVENTILATE PROPHYLACTICALLY
↓PaCO₂ → cerebral vasoconstriction → ↓ICP ✓ but also → ↓CBF → cerebral ischaemia ✗

Especially dangerous in first 24–48 hours — CBF already critically low after TBI.

Only acceptable use: Acute herniation (blown pupil, Cushing's triad) as a bridge to definitive surgery — brief, targeted, then stop.

Target during acute herniation: PaCO₂ 30–35 mmHg — NOT below 30 mmHg.

💉 Ketamine in TBI — Myth vs Evidence

Old teaching: Ketamine contraindicated in TBI — feared to raise ICP via sympathomimetic stimulation.

Current evidence: Ketamine does NOT raise ICP when the patient is ventilated. Multiple studies + meta-analyses support safety.

Advantages in TBI + haemodynamic instability:
· Maintains BP — preserves CPP
· Bronchodilatory — useful if bronchospasm
· Analgesic + amnestic

Conclusion: Ketamine is a reasonable RSI induction agent in TBI, especially when haemodynamically compromised. Be ready to defend this in the viva.

🚫 Steroids in TBI

CRASH trial (2004): Methylprednisolone in severe TBI → increased mortality at 2 weeks (RR 1.18).

Corticosteroids are CONTRAINDICATED in TBI. Do not give dexamethasone or methylprednisolone for ICP management.

⚡ TBI + Haemorrhagic Shock — The Hardest Scenario

Two competing demands:

· TBI needs MAP ≥80 mmHg (CPP preservation)
· Haemorrhage traditionally = permissive hypotension

Resolution:
1. Permissive hypotension has NO role when TBI is present
2. Target MAP ≥80 mmHg — accept the higher resuscitation target
3. Achieve haemorrhage control as fast as possible — damage control surgery
4. Do not delay haemorrhage control for ICP management
5. Once bleeding controlled — optimise CPP aggressively

🎯 Examiner Pearls

Q: What is Cushing's Triad and what does it mean?
Hypertension + Bradycardia + Irregular respiration = Cushing's Triad. Indicates critically raised ICP with brainstem compression — hypertension is a reflex to maintain CPP. Late and ominous sign. Treat as acute herniation: hyperventilate briefly, mannitol/HTS, urgent surgery.
Q: DECRA vs RESCUE-ICP — what did they show?
DECRA (2011): Early decompressive craniectomy reduced ICP but worsened unfavourable outcomes. RESCUE-ICP (2016): Late craniectomy reduced mortality but increased vegetative survival. Both show craniectomy is a last resort — it saves lives but at a neurological cost.
Q: Why avoid hypotonic fluids in TBI?
Hypotonic fluids (0.45% NaCl, 5% dextrose) lower serum osmolality → free water moves into brain cells → cerebral oedema → ↑ICP. Use isotonic (0.9% NaCl, Hartmann's) or hypertonic saline. Never hypotonic.
Q: Mannitol vs hypertonic saline — which when?
Mannitol: Works via osmotic diuresis — reduces ICP. Risk: causes diuresis → hypovolaemia → ↓BP → ↓CPP. Avoid if haemodynamically unstable.
Hypertonic saline: Raises osmolality WITHOUT diuresis — preserves intravascular volume. Preferred in haemodynamically unstable TBI.
Q: What is the FOUR score?
Full Outline of UnResponsiveness — alternative to GCS in intubated patients (GCS verbal component unusable). Assesses: Eye response (0–4) + Motor response (0–4) + Brainstem reflexes (0–4) + Breathing (0–4). Max 16. Better predicts outcome in ventilated patients.
