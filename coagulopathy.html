Coagulopathy in Trauma

ATC · RAC · DIC · TEG/ROTEM · Cascade vs Cell-Based Theory

🩸 Three Distinct Coagulopathies

① EARLY — PRE-RESUSCITATION
Acute Traumatic Coagulopathy (ATC)
Trigger: Tissue injury + systemic hypoperfusion — BOTH required.
Mechanism: Thrombomodulin ↑ → Protein C activation → inactivates Factors Va + VIIIa → ↓ thrombin + ↑ fibrinolysis (tPA from damaged glycocalyx).
Onset: Within 30 minutes. Incidence 25% of major trauma. 4× mortality.
Rx: TXA ≤3h · 1:1:1 MTP · Correct lethal triad
② LATER — RESUSCITATION-DRIVEN
Resuscitation-Associated Coagulopathy (RAC)
Trigger: Excessive crystalloid resuscitation.
Mechanisms: Dilution of clotting factors · Hypothermia (unwarmed fluids) → impairs enzyme kinetics · Hyperchloraemic acidosis → impairs coagulation · ↑ hydrostatic pressure disrupts clots.
Prevention: 1:1:1 blood products. Avoid large crystalloid. Warm all fluids.
③ LATE — CONSUMPTION
Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation (DIC)
Trigger: Massive tissue injury, sepsis, prolonged shock, amniotic fluid embolism.
Mechanism: Uncontrolled systemic thrombin → widespread microthrombi → consumption of factors, fibrinogen AND platelets → paradoxical bleeding.
Unlike ATC: Platelets LOW · Fibrinogen LOW · Microthrombi present · Organ failure.
Rx: Treat cause · FFP · Cryoprecipitate · Platelets

⚗️ Coagulation Theory — Cascade vs Cell-Based

🏛️ CLASSIC CASCADE MODEL
(Macfarlane 1964)
Two parallel pathways converging on Factor X:

INTRINSIC (contact)
XII → XI → IX + VIII → X

EXTRINSIC (tissue factor)
VII + TF → X

COMMON PATHWAY
X + V → Prothrombin → Thrombin → Fibrinogen → Fibrin

Limitation: Explains lab tests (PT, APTT) but does NOT reflect in vivo coagulation. Patients with haemophilia A (Factor VIII deficiency) bleed despite normal extrinsic pathway — cascade model cannot explain this.
🔬 CELL-BASED MODEL
(Hoffman & Monroe 2001)
Coagulation occurs on cell surfaces in 3 overlapping phases:

INITIATION (TF-bearing cells)
TF + VIIa → small amount of thrombin generated on TF-bearing cells (fibroblasts, monocytes)

AMPLIFICATION (platelets)
Small thrombin burst activates platelets + Factors V, VIII, XI → platelet surface primed

PROPAGATION (platelet surface)
Tenase + prothrombinase complexes on platelet surface → massive thrombin burst → stable fibrin clot

Why it matters: Explains why platelets are central · Why TXA works · Why TEG/ROTEM reflects reality better than PT/APTT · Basis for 1:1:1 MTP
Key clinical implication: The cell-based model explains why standard lab tests (PT, APTT) are inadequate for guiding trauma resuscitation — they measure individual pathway activity in plasma, not whole blood clot formation on cell surfaces. This is why TEG/ROTEM (viscoelastic testing) was developed — it measures the whole process.

ROTEM — THE 4 CHANNELS
EXTEM — Extrinsic Pathway
Activator: Tissue factor (TF) — triggers extrinsic pathway
What it measures: Overall clot formation — factors VII, X, V, II, fibrinogen, platelets
Abnormal CT: Factor deficiency (VII, X, V, II) → give FFP
Abnormal MCF: Platelet or fibrinogen deficiency → check FIBTEM to differentiate
Clinical use: First test to run — gives overall picture
INTEM — Intrinsic Pathway
Activator: Ellagic acid (contact activator) — triggers intrinsic pathway
What it measures: Factors XII, XI, IX, VIII, X, V, II, fibrinogen, platelets
Key use: Detects heparin effect — compare INTEM vs HEPTEM
Abnormal CT vs normal HEPTEM CT: Confirms heparin is prolonging clotting
Clinical use: Monitors heparin anticoagulation in cardiac surgery/ECMO
FIBTEM — Fibrinogen Channel
Activator: TF + cytochalasin D (platelet inhibitor)
What it measures: Fibrinogen contribution to clot strength ONLY — platelets blocked
Normal MCF: 9–25 mm
Abnormal MCF <8mm: Fibrinogen deficiency → give cryoprecipitate or fibrinogen concentrate
Key interpretation: Compare EXTEM MCF vs FIBTEM MCF:
— ↓EXTEM + normal FIBTEM = platelet problem → give platelets
— ↓EXTEM + ↓FIBTEM = fibrinogen problem → give cryoprecipitate
FIBTEM is the most clinically useful channel in trauma
HEPTEM — Heparin Channel
Activator: Ellagic acid + heparinase (neutralises heparin)
What it measures: Intrinsic pathway with heparin effect removed
Key interpretation: Compare INTEM CT vs HEPTEM CT:
— INTEM CT prolonged + HEPTEM CT normal = heparin present → give protamine
— Both prolonged = factor deficiency, not heparin → give FFP
Clinical use: Post-cardiac surgery, heparin reversal monitoring, ECMO

💀 Diamond of Death

MUTUALLY REINFORCING — EACH WORSENS THE OTHERS
🥶 Hypothermia
<34°C impairs enzyme kinetics. Worsens acidosis. Target >36°C actively.
🧪 Acidosis
pH <7.1 = 70-80% ↓ factor activity. ↓ platelet function. BD >6 = marker.
🔬 Coagulopathy
ATC + dilution + hypothermia. More bleeding → more shock → more lethal triad.
🦴 Hypocalcaemia
Citrate chelates Ca²⁺. ↓ contractility + ↓ coagulation. CaCl₂ with every 4 units pRBC.

💊 Tranexamic Acid — CRASH-2

Mechanism: Inhibits plasminogen activation → antifibrinolytic → protects forming clot from premature lysis

Dose: 1g IV over 10 min → 1g IV over 8 hours

Window: ≤3 hours from injury. After 3h: fibrinolytic phase over — TXA may promote pathological microthrombi → ↑ mortality (CRASH-2).

CRASH-2: 20,211 patients — TXA ≤3h reduced all-cause mortality (RR 0.91). Benefit greatest ≤1h (RR 0.68).

Give TXA to ALL significant haemorrhage. Do not wait for coagulation results.

🎯 Examiner Pearls

Q: What does FIBTEM on ROTEM tell you that TEG cannot?
FIBTEM uses cytochalasin D to block platelet contribution — the resulting MCF reflects fibrinogen contribution alone to clot strength. This allows you to specifically diagnose fibrinogen deficiency vs platelet deficiency. TEG cannot separate these. FIBTEM MCF <8mm = give cryoprecipitate.
Q: Why does the cascade model fail to explain haemophilia A bleeding?
Haemophilia A = Factor VIII deficiency. In the cascade model, the extrinsic pathway (TF + VII) bypasses Factor VIII and should generate enough thrombin via the common pathway. Yet haemophiliacs bleed profusely. The cell-based model explains this: without Factor VIII, the amplification phase on the platelet surface fails → insufficient thrombin burst → inadequate stable clot.
Q: ROTEM shows ↓EXTEM MCF but normal FIBTEM MCF — what does this mean?
EXTEM measures overall clot strength (platelet + fibrinogen). FIBTEM measures fibrinogen only. Normal FIBTEM = fibrinogen is fine. ↓EXTEM MCF with normal FIBTEM = platelet deficiency is responsible for the weak clot. Treatment: give platelets.
Q: How does acidosis impair coagulation at the molecular level?
Coagulation enzymes are serine proteases — their active sites are pH-dependent. At pH 7.4: normal. pH 7.2: 50% reduction in factor activity. pH 7.0: 70–80% reduction. Acidosis also impairs fibrin polymerisation and platelet aggregation. This is why pouring more FFP into an acidic patient without correcting the acidosis is futile.
