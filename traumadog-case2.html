⬇ Management Flowchart — tap each step to expand

01 Identify Neurogenic Shock — Clinical Diagnosis
Bradycardia + hypotension = neurogenic shock until proven otherwise.

Cord injury at T7–T8 disrupts sympathetic outflow (T1–L2). Cardiac accelerator fibres from T1–T4 — injuries at or above T6 remove this input entirely. Vagal tone is unopposed → bradycardia.

Press your hand on the patient's thigh. Warm = neurogenic. Cold = haemorrhagic.

02 Vasopressor — Noradrenaline First-Line
Noradrenaline 0.1–0.2 mcg/kg/min — alpha-1 vasoconstriction restores SVR; mild beta-1 supports CO; does not worsen bradycardia.

Why NOT phenylephrine? Pure alpha-1 agonist → reflex bradycardia. Already bradycardic from vagal predominance — phenylephrine makes it worse.

Atropine 0.6 mg IV — effect modest and transient. The bradycardia is structural, not purely vagal excess.
tap to expand ▾
03 MAP Target — 85–90 mmHg for 7 Days
This is the most consequential management decision in acute SCI.

The injured cord is exquisitely sensitive to hypoperfusion. Secondary ischaemia at MAP 70 vs 85 mmHg causes preventable neurological deterioration independent of the primary injury.

NASCIS data and observational studies: MAP 85–90 mmHg for 7 days post-injury. Not a soft target — periods below 85 mmHg correlate with worsened neurological outcome.

04 Surgical Stabilisation — Day 2
Posterior stabilisation T5–T10 once haemodynamically stable.

Induction: propofol 120 mg + fentanyl 200 mcg.

⚠ SUXAMETHONIUM AVOIDED from 72h post-injury. Denervated muscle upregulates extrajunctional acetylcholine receptors → massive hyperkalaemic response → cardiac arrest. Use rocuronium 0.6 mg/kg.

Maintenance: TIVA (propofol + remifentanil) prone. Noradrenaline infusion continued throughout.

05 Spinal Shock vs Neurogenic Shock — The Key Distinction
Spinal shock: neurological phenomenon. Temporary flaccid paralysis + loss of all reflexes below injury immediately post-event. Resolves over days to weeks (return of bulbocavernosus reflex marks its end). Not haemodynamic.

Neurogenic shock: haemodynamic phenomenon. Loss of sympathetic vasomotor tone → hypotension + bradycardia + warm peripheries. Not neurological per se.

They can co-exist simultaneously. The exam loves this distinction.

📌 Key Teaching Points

❤️Bradycardia + hypotension after spinal trauma = neurogenic shock — haemorrhagic shock is ALWAYS tachycardic.
🌡️Neurogenic shock: warm peripheries below injury (absent sympathetic vasoconstriction).
💊Noradrenaline first-line. Phenylephrine worsens the bradycardia.
🎯MAP 85–90 mmHg for 7 days — secondary cord ischaemia is preventable.
🚫Never suxamethonium from 72h post-injury — hyperkalaemic arrest from upregulated extrajunctional receptors.
💧Large-volume crystalloid is harmful — treat vasodilation with vasopressors, not fluid loading.
🧠Spinal shock (neurological) ≠ Neurogenic shock (haemodynamic). They can co-exist.

🎤 Viva Questions

Why does neurogenic shock produce bradycardia while haemorrhagic shock produces tachycardia? ▶
Haemorrhagic shock activates the sympathetic nervous system via baroreceptor reflex → tachycardia and vasoconstriction. Neurogenic shock from cord injury above T6 disrupts sympathetic outflow entirely, including cardiac accelerator fibres from T1–T4. Vagal tone is now unopposed → bradycardia. The two mechanisms are physiologically opposite.
Why is the MAP target 85–90 mmHg rather than the usual 65 mmHg? ▶
The injured spinal cord loses its autoregulatory capacity and is entirely dependent on systemic MAP for perfusion pressure. At MAP 65 mmHg, the cord may be ischaemic. NASCIS observational data and SCI guidelines support MAP 85–90 mmHg for 7 days — this is the window when secondary ischaemic injury is most preventable. MAP 65 mmHg acceptable in sepsis is actively harmful in acute SCI.
When does suxamethonium become dangerous in SCI, and what is the mechanism? ▶
From approximately 72 hours post-injury, denervated muscle fibres upregulate extrajunctional nicotinic acetylcholine receptors across the entire muscle membrane. Suxamethonium binds all these receptors simultaneously → massive synchronised potassium efflux → acute hyperkalaemia → cardiac arrest. Risk persists 6–12 months or indefinitely in complete injuries. Rocuronium is safe.
Distinguish spinal shock from neurogenic shock. ▶
Spinal shock is neurological: sudden loss of all cord reflexes below injury immediately post-event — flaccid paralysis, areflexia, loss of bladder/bowel tone. Resolves over days to weeks. The return of the bulbocavernosus reflex marks its end.

Neurogenic shock is haemodynamic: loss of sympathetic vasomotor tone → hypotension, bradycardia, warm peripheries. Both can co-exist in the same patient.
