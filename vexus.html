VEXUS Score

Venous Excess Ultrasound · Grading Venous Congestion
VEXUS grades systemic venous congestion using IVC diameter plus Doppler waveforms in three separate organs — used to identify congestive/cardiorenal AKI and guide de-resuscitation decisions in fluid-overloaded, right-heart-failure, or over-resuscitated trauma/ICU patients.

🎯 Entry Criterion — When to Proceed

Assess IVC diameter first. IVC ≥2cm (dilated) → proceed to grade the three Doppler waveforms. If IVC is small/normal, congestion is unlikely — VEXUS grading not needed.

📡 Three Doppler Waveforms Assessed

Hepatic Vein — Normal vs Congested
Normal: S-wave (systolic) > D-wave (diastolic) forward flow. Worsening congestion → S-wave blunts, then reverses (flows backward during systole) — most severe hepatic vein finding.
Portal Vein — Continuous vs Pulsatile
Pulsatility fraction = (Vmax−Vmin)/Vmax. Normal: minimal pulsatility, steady flow. Mild: 30-50%. Severe: >50% — cardiac pulsation transmitted through the congested liver into the portal system.
Renal Vein — Continuous → Biphasic → Monophasic
Monophasic = worst grade. Diastolic flow is lost first — during diastole, right-sided pressures peak and are transmitted backward, halting renal venous outflow. Systole represents the one recurring pressure "dip" that still permits flow. As congestion worsens further, even this narrows.

🔬 Why Monophasic Renal Vein = Congestive AKI

The Mechanism
Elevated CVP transmits backward through IVC → renal vein → renal parenchyma → raises renal interstitial pressure.
This directly opposes the glomerular filtration pressure gradient (GFR depends on glomerular capillary pressure minus Bowman's capsule/interstitial pressure).
The kidney is effectively compressed by its own back-pressure — congestive/cardiorenal AKI, mechanistically opposite to classic hypovolaemic AKI.
Clinical implication: giving more fluid in this state worsens rather than improves urine output — the opposite instinct to volume-depletion AKI.

🎯 Clinical Applications

• Identifying congestive AKI in over-resuscitated trauma/ICU patients — distinguishing from hypovolaemic AKI
• Guiding de-resuscitation/diuresis decisions — objective marker beyond fluid balance charts alone
• Right heart failure/tamponade assessment — congestion severity tracking
• Post-massive-transfusion patients transitioning from resuscitation to de-resuscitation phase (links directly to your DCR "phase 3" concept)

🎯 Examiner Pearls

Q: What's the entry criterion before grading VEXUS severity?
IVC diameter ≥2cm. If the IVC is not dilated, significant venous congestion is unlikely and further Doppler grading is not required.
Q: Why does diastolic renal venous flow disappear before systolic flow as congestion worsens?
Diastole is when right-sided venous pressures peak (tricuspid valve open, RA/RV pressures rising) — in a congested state this pressure surge is transmitted backward and halts forward renal venous outflow. Systole (tricuspid closed, RA relaxing) represents a transient pressure "dip" that preserves some forward flow longer — hence biphasic (diastole lost first) precedes monophasic (only systolic flow remains) as the worst grade.
Q: Portal vein pulsatility fraction formula?
(Vmax − Vmin) / Vmax. Mild congestion: 30-50%. Severe: >50%. Reflects cardiac pulsation being transmitted through a congested liver into the normally steady-flow portal system.
